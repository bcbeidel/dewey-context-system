# Systematic Review: Strengths & Limitations

**Purpose**: Understanding when systematic-review works best and what challenges to expect.

---

## What Works Well

### Strengths
- Systematic comparison of research studies
- Identifying consensus and contradictions across literature
- Tracking theoretical development over time
- Finding research gaps and future directions
- Creating publication-ready reviews
- PRISMA 2020 compliance for academic rigor

### Optimal Conditions
- Access to academic papers (PDFs or full-text)
- Clear research question (PICO framework)
- 5-20 papers (manageable scope)
- Papers in similar domain
- Mix of methodologies (RCTs, meta-analyses, qualitative)
- Well-defined inclusion/exclusion criteria

---

## Limitations

### Challenges
- **Access barriers**: Paywalled papers (user must provide access)
- **Technical complexity**: Highly technical statistical analysis requires expertise
- **Domain expertise**: Domain-specific jargon requires user interpretation
- **Scale issues**: Very large reviews (50+ papers) become unwieldy
- **Time investment**: Comprehensive reviews take 8-12 hours

### Workarounds
- **Paper access**: Use Google Scholar, ResearchGate, author websites, library access
- **Statistics**: Focus on practical interpretation for complex stats; consult statistician if needed
- **Large reviews**: Break into sub-topics or phases (e.g., by methodology, time period)
- **Reference management**: Use Zotero/Mendeley for massive projects (100+ papers)
- **Expertise gaps**: Collaborate with domain experts for technical validation

---

## When to Use This Skill

**Best fit:**
- Academic research projects (thesis, dissertation)
- Publication-ready literature reviews
- Grant proposals requiring rigorous review
- Evidence-based practice guidelines
- Research gap analysis

**Not ideal for:**
- Quick background research (use WebSearch instead)
- Non-academic sources (use /research-synthesis instead)
- Single paper summaries (use Read tool)
- Opinion pieces or editorials
