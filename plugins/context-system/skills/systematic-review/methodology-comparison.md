# Methodology Comparison & Findings Synthesis

**Purpose**: Compare research methods across studies and synthesize findings thematically (not paper-by-paper).

**When to use**: After completing individual paper extractions.

---

## Step 1: Create Methodology Comparison Table

Systematically compare research approaches across all reviewed papers.

### Comparison Table Format

```markdown
## Methodology Comparison

| Study | Method | Sample | Key Finding | Effect Size | Limitations |
|-------|--------|--------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Smith (2020) | RCT | N=120 undergrads | SR improved retention 40% | d=0.82 | Short-term only |
| Jones (2021) | Meta-analysis | 45 studies | SR effective across ages | Overall d=0.67 | Publication bias |
| Lee (2022) | Longitudinal | N=500, 2 years | SR benefits persist | d=0.55 at 12mo | Self-reported |
```

### Analysis Questions

After creating the table, analyze:

**Methodological Patterns**:
- What methods are most common?
- Do different methods yield different results?
- Which studies have strongest designs?
- What sample sizes are typical?

**Result Patterns**:
- Do findings converge or diverge?
- Are effect sizes consistent?
- Do longitudinal and cross-sectional studies agree?
- Which findings are most robust?

**Quality Assessment**:
- Which studies have highest internal validity?
- Which have best external validity?
- What are common methodological limitations?
- Are there systematic biases?

## Step 2: Thematic Findings Synthesis

**CRITICAL**: Organize by theme, NOT by paper. This is what distinguishes synthesis from summary.

### Theme Organization

Identify 3-5 major themes across papers:
- Areas of consensus
- Areas of debate
- Emerging patterns
- Unexpected findings

### Synthesis Template

```markdown
## Synthesized Findings

### Theme 1: [Theme Name]

**Consensus/Pattern**: [What do studies agree on?]

**Evidence**:
- Author et al. (Year): [Finding with effect size/stats] (Method, N=X)
- Author et al. (Year): [Finding with effect size/stats] (Method, N=X)
- Author et al. (Year): [Finding with effect size/stats] (Method, N=X)

**Effect Size Range**: [Range across studies, e.g., d=0.45 to d=0.90]

**Moderators**: [What factors influence the effect?]
- Moderator 1: [How it affects results]
- Moderator 2: [How it affects results]

**Strength of Evidence**: [Strong | Moderate | Weak | Mixed]

---

### Theme 2: [Theme Name]

**Mixed Evidence**: [Describe conflicting findings]

**Position A** (N studies):
- [Description of position]
- Citations: Author (Year), Author (Year), Author (Year)
- Evidence quality: [Assessment]

**Position B** (N studies):
- [Description of opposing position]
- Citations: Author (Year), Author (Year), Author (Year)
- Evidence quality: [Assessment]

**Possible Explanations for Divergence**:
- Methodological differences: [Details]
- Population differences: [Details]
- Contextual factors: [Details]

**Resolution/Current Thinking**: [How field addresses this contradiction]

---

### Theme 3: [Theme Name]

**Emerging Pattern**: [New or under-studied finding]

**Evidence**:
- [List studies supporting this pattern]
- [Note if evidence is preliminary]

**Gap**: [What's missing to strengthen this finding?]

**Implications**: [Why this matters]
```

## Synthesis Best Practices

### DO: Organize Thematically
```
✅ GOOD: Theme-Based Organization

### Theme: Effectiveness of Intervention
Smith (2020), Jones (2021), and Lee (2022) all found...
However, Rodriguez (2023) found different results when...
```

```
❌ BAD: Paper-by-Paper Summary

### Smith (2020)
Found that...

### Jones (2021)
Found that...

### Lee (2022)
Found that...
```

### DO: Compare Across Studies
- Note where studies agree (consensus)
- Note where studies disagree (debate)
- Identify methodological reasons for differences
- Look for patterns in effect sizes

### DO: Use Evidence Grading
- **Strong evidence**: Multiple high-quality studies, consistent findings, large effects
- **Moderate evidence**: Some studies, mostly consistent, medium effects
- **Weak evidence**: Few studies, mixed quality, small or inconsistent effects
- **Insufficient evidence**: Too few studies or too inconsistent to draw conclusions

### DO: Include Effect Sizes
When available, always report:
- Individual study effect sizes
- Range across studies
- Average/median effect size
- Context for interpretation (small, medium, large)

## Common Synthesis Patterns

### Pattern 1: Strong Consensus
All or nearly all studies find the same result.

**Example**:
> All 12 reviewed studies found spaced repetition superior to massed practice for long-term retention (effect size range: d=0.52 to d=0.90). This represents strong consensus in the literature.

### Pattern 2: Qualified Consensus
Studies agree with important moderators.

**Example**:
> Studies consistently show benefits, but effect varies by learner age. Larger effects in younger learners (d=0.78, 5 studies) compared to older adults (d=0.45, 3 studies).

### Pattern 3: Methodological Divergence
Different methods yield different results.

**Example**:
> Lab-based RCTs show large effects (d=0.85), but real-world implementation studies show smaller effects (d=0.48), suggesting efficacy-effectiveness gap.

### Pattern 4: Temporal Evolution
Understanding has changed over time.

**Example**:
> Early studies (pre-2015) focused on frequency; recent work (2020+) emphasizes timing and context, leading to more nuanced understanding.

- [ ] Consensus areas identified

- [ ] Moderating factors identified
- [ ] Methodological patterns noted
