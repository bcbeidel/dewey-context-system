# Evidence Synthesis & Systematic Review Standards

**Purpose**: Best practices for systematic reviews and evidence synthesis based on PRISMA 2020, Cochrane Handbook, and rapid evidence assessment methods

**Created**: 2026-02-08
**Status**: Research synthesis for `/researcher` skill implementation

---

## Overview

Evidence synthesis encompasses methods for systematically identifying, evaluating, and integrating research findings to answer specific questions. The gold standard is the systematic review following PRISMA 2020 guidelines.

**Core principle**: Transparency and reproducibility through comprehensive documentation of search strategies, screening decisions, data extraction, and synthesis methods.

---

## PRISMA 2020 Statement

### Purpose and Scope

**PRISMA** (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic reviews and Meta-Analyses) is an evidence-based minimum set of items for reporting in systematic reviews and meta-analyses.

**Updated 2020**: Expands from 27 to 27 items with clearer guidance, additional detail, and explicit reporting of automated tools, risk of bias assessment, and certainty of evidence.

**When to use**: All systematic reviews of studies evaluating health interventions, plus broadly applicable to reviews in other fields.

**Source**: [PRISMA 2020 Statement](https://www.prisma-statement.org/prisma-2020-statement) - Page, M. J., et al. (2021). The PRISMA 2020 statement: An updated guideline for reporting systematic reviews. BMJ, 372, n71.

---

### PRISMA 2020 Checklist (27 Items)

**Title and Abstract** (2 items):
1. Title: Identify as systematic review
2. Abstract: Structured summary (background, methods, results, conclusions)

**Introduction** (3 items):
3. Rationale: Describe rationale for review in context of existing knowledge
4. Objectives: Provide explicit statement of objectives or questions (PICO format)

**Methods** (14 items):
5. Eligibility criteria: Specify inclusion/exclusion criteria with rationale
6. Information sources: Specify all sources and dates searched
7. Search strategy: Present full search strategy for at least one database
8. Selection process: Describe screening and selection process
9. Data collection process: Describe methods for data extraction and deviations
10. Data items: List and define all outcomes/variables collected
11. Study risk of bias assessment: Specify methods used
12. Effect measures: Specify effect measures used
13. Synthesis methods: Describe methods for synthesis and exploration of heterogeneity
14. Reporting bias assessment: Describe methods (publication bias, selective reporting)
15. Certainty assessment: Methods for assessing certainty of evidence

**Results** (8 items):
16. Study selection: Provide flow diagram and reasons for exclusions
17. Study characteristics: Cite and present characteristics of included studies
18. Risk of bias: Present assessments per study
19. Results of individual studies: Present results for all outcomes
20. Results of syntheses: Present results of meta-analysis or other synthesis
21. Reporting biases: Present assessment results
22. Certainty of evidence: Present certainty ratings per outcome

**Discussion** (4 items):
23. Discussion: Interpret results in context of other evidence
24. Limitations: Discuss limitations at study and outcome level
25. Conclusions: Provide general interpretation in context of other evidence
26. Funding: Specify funding sources for review
27. Competing interests: Declare any conflicts of interest

**Source**: [PRISMA 2020 Checklist](https://www.prisma-statement.org/prisma-2020-checklist)

---

### PRISMA 2020 Flow Diagram

**Purpose**: Visual representation of screening and selection process

**Components**:
- **Identification**: Records identified from databases, registers, other sources
- **Screening**: Records screened, records excluded
- **Included**: Studies included in review, studies included in meta-analysis

**Reporting**: Must report numbers at each stage and reasons for exclusions

## PRISMA-P 2015 (Protocols)

### Purpose

**PRISMA-P**: Reporting guideline for systematic review protocols to improve transparency and reduce risk of bias from post-hoc decisions.

**Protocol registration**: Strongly recommended on [PROSPERO](https://www.crd.york.ac.uk/prospero/) registry before starting review.

**Why protocols matter**:
- Prevents outcome switching (selective reporting)
- Makes deviations transparent
- Enables assessment of publication bias
- Allows others to see reviews in progress (avoid duplication)

**Source**: [PRISMA-P 2015](https://systematicreviewsjournal.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/2046-4053-4-1) - Moher, D., et al. (2015). Preferred reporting items for systematic review and meta-analysis protocols (PRISMA-P) 2015 statement. Systematic Reviews, 4, 1.

---

### PRISMA-P Checklist (17 Items)

**Administrative Information** (7 items):
1. Title: Identify as protocol for systematic review
2. Registration: Provide PROSPERO registration number
3. Authors: Provide contact details
4. Amendments: Document amendments to protocol
5. Support: Describe sources of support
6. Review team: List review team members and roles

**Introduction** (3 items):
7. Rationale: Describe rationale in context
8. Objectives: Provide explicit objectives (PICO)
9. Eligibility: Specify criteria

**Methods** (7 items):
10. Information sources: Describe sources
11. Search strategy: Present draft search strategy
12. Study records: Describe screening and data management
13. Data items: List and define planned data to collect
14. Outcomes: List primary and secondary outcomes
15. Risk of bias: Specify planned assessment methods
16. Data synthesis: Specify planned synthesis methods
17. Meta-bias: Specify planned assessment of publication bias

**Source**: [PRISMA-P Checklist](https://www.prisma-statement.org/documents/PRISMA-P-checklist.pdf)

## Quality Assessment: AMSTAR-2

### Overview

**AMSTAR-2** (A MeaSurement Tool to Assess systematic Reviews) is a critical appraisal tool for systematic reviews that include randomized or non-randomized studies of healthcare interventions.

**Purpose**: Assess methodological quality of systematic reviews

**16 domains**: Each rated Yes/Partial Yes/No

**Quality rating**: High/Moderate/Low/Critically low

**Source**: [AMSTAR-2 Tool](https://amstar.ca/Amstar-2.php) - Shea, B. J., et al. (2017). AMSTAR 2: A critical appraisal tool for systematic reviews. BMJ, 358, j4008.

---

### AMSTAR-2 Domains

**Critical domains** (must be satisfied for high/moderate rating):
2. Protocol registered before commencement
4. Adequate literature search
7. List of excluded studies with justification
9. Risk of bias assessment used
11. Appropriate meta-analysis methods (if used)
13. Account for risk of bias in interpretation
15. Assessment of publication bias

**Non-critical domains** (important but not disqualifying):
1. PICO components in question
3. Study design inclusion explained
5. Duplicate study selection and data extraction
6. List of included studies with characteristics
8. Description of included studies
10. Funding sources of included studies reported
12. Assessment of impact of risk of bias
14. Explanation of heterogeneity
16. Conflicts of interest disclosed

**Rating thresholds**:
- **High**: No critical flaws, ≤1 non-critical flaw
- **Moderate**: No critical flaws, >1 non-critical flaw
- **Low**: 1 critical flaw, with/without non-critical flaws
- **Critically low**: >1 critical flaw, with/without non-critical flaws

## Risk of Bias: ROBIS

### Overview

**ROBIS** (Risk Of Bias In Systematic reviews) is a tool for assessing risk of bias in systematic reviews.

**Purpose**: Identify concerns about bias in review process and findings

**Three phases**:
1. Assess relevance (optional): Is review relevant to your question?
2. Identify concerns with review process (4 domains)
3. Judge overall risk of bias

**Source**: [ROBIS Tool](https://www.bristol.ac.uk/population-health-sciences/projects/robis/) - Whiting, P., et al. (2016). ROBIS: A new tool to assess risk of bias in systematic reviews. BMJ, 354, i4919.

---

### ROBIS Domains

**Phase 2: Identify concerns (4 domains)**:

**Domain 1: Study eligibility criteria**
- Concerns: Were criteria appropriate? Did restrictions introduce bias?

**Domain 2: Identification and selection of studies**
- Concerns: Were search methods likely to retrieve relevant studies?

**Domain 3: Data collection and study appraisal**
- Concerns: Were data collected accurately? Was risk of bias assessed appropriately?

**Domain 4: Synthesis and findings**
- Concerns: Was synthesis appropriate given data? Were findings robust?

**Phase 3: Overall risk of bias**
- Low / High / Unclear
- Consider all domains together

## Cochrane Handbook

### Overview

**Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions** is the definitive guide to conducting systematic reviews of health interventions.

**Latest version**: Version 6.4 (2023)

**Covers**: All aspects from protocol development through publication

**Particularly strong on**: Risk of bias assessment (RoB 2 tool), meta-analysis methods, GRADE certainty assessment

**Source**: [Cochrane Handbook](https://training.cochrane.org/handbook) - Higgins, J. P. T., et al. (2023). Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions version 6.4.

---

### Key Cochrane Contributions

**RoB 2 Tool** (Risk of Bias 2.0):
- Domain-based evaluation for randomized trials
- Five domains: randomization, deviations, missing data, measurement, selection
- Algorithm produces Low/Some concerns/High risk judgments

**GRADE** (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation):
- Framework for rating certainty of evidence
- Four levels: High/Moderate/Low/Very low
- Five factors decrease certainty (risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, publication bias)
- Three factors increase certainty (large effect, dose-response, plausible confounding)

**Meta-analysis guidance**:
- Statistical methods for combining results
- Handling heterogeneity (I² statistic, subgroup analysis, meta-regression)
- Sensitivity analyses

## Rapid Evidence Assessment (REA)

### Overview

**REA** is a pragmatic variant of systematic review designed for time-constrained contexts while maintaining methodological rigor.

**Purpose**: Provide evidence synthesis when full systematic review is not feasible due to time or resource constraints

**Timeline**: 1-2 weeks (vs. 4-8 weeks for full systematic review)

**Trade-offs**: Narrower scope, fewer databases, single reviewer screening, abbreviated protocol

**Source**: Based on UK government and academic practice in rapid evidence synthesis

---

### REA vs. Full Systematic Review

| Aspect | Full Systematic Review | Rapid Evidence Assessment |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| **Timeline** | 4-8 weeks | 1-2 weeks |
| **Protocol** | Registered (PROSPERO) | Documented (internal) |
| **Search** | Comprehensive (5-10 databases) | Targeted (2-3 key databases) |
| **Screening** | Duplicate reviewers | Single reviewer + spot checks |
| **Quality Assessment** | Full AMSTAR-2/ROBIS | Abbreviated checklist |
| **Synthesis** | Extensive thematic + meta-analysis | Thematic synthesis only |
| **Documentation** | Full PRISMA 2020 compliance | Simplified reporting |

---

### When to Use REA

**✅ Use REA when**:
- Time constraints (business decisions, consulting projects)
- Sufficient research exists (10-30 relevant papers)
- Question is relatively focused
- Exploratory synthesis to inform full review
- Resource constraints (small team, limited access)

**❌ Use full systematic review when**:
- Publication in academic journal
- High-stakes policy decisions
- Medical/clinical practice guidelines
- Contested or controversial questions
- Grant/protocol requires PRISMA compliance

## Search Strategies

### Comprehensive Search (Full Systematic Review)

**Databases** (minimum 3-5):
- Academic: PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, PsycINFO
- Grey literature: Google Scholar, OpenGrey, ProQuest Dissertations

**Search components**:
- Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT)
- MeSH terms (controlled vocabulary)
- Truncation and wildcards
- Field restrictions (title, abstract, keywords)

**Supplementary methods**:
- Reference list screening (backward citation)
- Citation tracking (forward citation)
- Hand-searching key journals
- Expert consultation

**Documentation**: Report complete search strategy for at least one database (PRISMA #7)

---

### Targeted Search (REA)

**Databases** (2-3 focused):
- Primary: Google Scholar (broad coverage)
- Secondary: PubMed or Scopus (depending on field)
- Optional: Field-specific database

**Search strategy**:
- Core keywords only (skip extensive synonym lists)
- Recent literature focus (last 5-10 years)
- English language only (if appropriate)
- Supplement with highly cited papers

**Time-saving**: Skip grey literature, reduce citation tracking, focus on seminal works

---

## Screening and Selection

### Dual Screening (Full Systematic Review)

**Process**:
1. Title/abstract screening: Two reviewers independently
2. Discrepancies: Resolve through discussion or third reviewer
3. Full-text screening: Two reviewers independently
4. Final inclusion: Consensus process

**Inter-rater reliability**: Calculate Cohen's kappa (target: κ > 0.60)

**Tools**: Covidence, Rayyan, DistillerSR for screening management

---

### Single Screening with Spot Checks (REA)

**Process**:
1. Title/abstract screening: Single reviewer
2. Spot check: Second reviewer validates 10-20% random sample
3. Full-text screening: Single reviewer
4. Validation: Discuss borderline cases with team

**Quality check**: If spot check agreement <80%, expand dual screening

## Data Extraction

### Systematic Data Extraction

**Standard fields**:
- **Study metadata**: Authors, year, journal, DOI
- **Study design**: Method, sample, setting, duration
- **Participants**: N, demographics, inclusion/exclusion applied
- **Interventions/exposures**: Description, dose, duration, comparison
- **Outcomes**: Measures, time points, effect sizes, confidence intervals
- **Risk of bias**: Domain ratings, overall assessment
- **Funding**: Sources, conflicts of interest

**Process**:
- Pilot extraction form with 2-3 studies
- Dual extraction (at least 10% of studies)
- Validate extracted data against original papers

**Tools**: Standardized forms, Excel/CSV, Covidence

## Quality and Risk of Bias Assessment

### Quality Assessment Tools

**For systematic reviews**:
- AMSTAR-2 (16 items, critical appraisal)
- ROBIS (4 domains, risk of bias)

**For primary studies**:
- RoB 2 (randomized controlled trials) - Cochrane tool
- ROBINS-I (non-randomized interventions)
- Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (observational studies)
- CASP Checklists (qualitative studies)

**Best practice**: Pre-specify quality assessment method in protocol, assess all included studies, use in sensitivity analysis

## Synthesis Methods

### Narrative Synthesis

**Purpose**: Thematic organization of findings when meta-analysis not appropriate

**Process**:
1. Group studies by theme, intervention, population
2. Identify patterns and relationships
3. Assess robustness (quality, consistency)
4. Explore heterogeneity (why do findings differ?)

**When to use**: Heterogeneous studies, insufficient data for meta-analysis, qualitative synthesis

---

### Meta-Analysis

**Purpose**: Statistical combination of quantitative results from multiple studies

**Requirements**:
- Comparable outcomes across studies
- Sufficient homogeneity (or planned subgroup analysis)
- Adequate reporting of effect sizes

**Common models**:
- Fixed-effect: Assumes true effect is same across studies
- Random-effects: Allows true effect to vary across studies

**Heterogeneity**: Assess with I² statistic (>50% = substantial heterogeneity)

**Reporting**: Forest plots, effect estimates, confidence intervals, p-values

**Source**: Cochrane Handbook Chapter 10 (Meta-analysis)

---

## Reporting Biases

### Publication Bias

**Definition**: Tendency for studies with positive results to be published more than negative results

**Assessment methods**:
- Funnel plots (visual inspection)
- Egger's test (statistical)
- Trim and fill analysis
- Fail-safe N calculation

**Mitigation**: Include grey literature, pre-registered protocols, search trial registries

---

### Selective Reporting Bias

**Definition**: Selective reporting of outcomes or analyses within published studies

**Detection**: Compare protocol to published results, check trial registries

**Impact**: Can inflate effect sizes, mislead conclusions

## Certainty of Evidence (GRADE)

### GRADE Framework

**Purpose**: Rate certainty (confidence) in evidence for each outcome

**Four levels**:
- **High**: Very confident in effect estimate
- **Moderate**: Moderately confident; true effect likely close to estimate
- **Low**: Limited confidence; true effect may differ substantially
- **Very low**: Very little confidence; true effect likely substantially different

**Starting point**: RCTs start High, observational studies start Low

**Source**: [GRADE Handbook](https://gdt.gradepro.org/app/handbook/handbook.html)

---

### Factors Decreasing Certainty

1. **Risk of bias**: Limitations in study design
2. **Inconsistency**: Unexplained heterogeneity
3. **Indirectness**: Evidence from different population/intervention/outcome
4. **Imprecision**: Wide confidence intervals, small sample sizes
5. **Publication bias**: Evidence of selective publication

Each factor can decrease certainty by 1-2 levels

---

### Factors Increasing Certainty (Observational Studies)

1. **Large effect**: Strong association (RR >2 or <0.5)
2. **Dose-response gradient**: Clear relationship
3. **All plausible confounding would reduce effect**: Conservative bias

Each factor can increase certainty by 1 level (only for observational studies starting at Low)

## Timeline Estimates

### Full Systematic Review (PRISMA 2020 Compliant)

**Total**: 4-8 weeks

- **Protocol development**: 1 week
- **PROSPERO registration**: 1-3 weeks (external review)
- **Literature search**: 3-5 days
- **Screening (dual)**: 1-2 weeks
- **Data extraction (dual)**: 1-2 weeks
- **Quality assessment**: 3-5 days
- **Synthesis**: 1 week
- **Manuscript preparation**: 1-2 weeks

**Team**: Minimum 2 reviewers (screening, extraction), ideally 3-4 (add arbiter, statistician)

---

### Rapid Evidence Assessment

**Total**: 1-2 weeks

- **Protocol documentation**: 1 day
- **Literature search**: 1-2 days
- **Screening (single + spot check)**: 2-3 days
- **Data extraction**: 3-5 days
- **Quality check**: 1 day
- **Synthesis**: 2-3 days
- **Report preparation**: 2-3 days

**Team**: 1-2 reviewers (primary reviewer + validator)

---

## Quality Criteria

### High-Quality Systematic Review Demonstrates

**Protocol**:
- Pre-registered protocol (PROSPERO)
- Clear PICO question
- Pre-specified outcomes and analyses

**Search**:
- Comprehensive search (≥3 databases)
- Documented search strategy
- Grey literature included

**Screening**:
- Duplicate screening
- PRISMA flow diagram
- Reasons for exclusions documented

**Risk of bias**:
- Appropriate tool used (RoB 2, ROBINS-I)
- All studies assessed
- Impact on findings discussed

**Synthesis**:
- Appropriate method (narrative or meta-analysis)
- Heterogeneity explored
- Sensitivity analyses conducted

**Reporting**:
- PRISMA 2020 checklist complete
- Limitations acknowledged
- Certainty of evidence assessed (GRADE)

## When to Use Evidence Synthesis

**✅ Use systematic review when**:
- Academic research (thesis, dissertation)
- Policy or clinical guidelines
- Grant applications
- Publication in peer-reviewed journals
- High-stakes decisions requiring transparency

**✅ Use REA when**:
- Business or consulting projects
- Preliminary exploration before full review
- Time-constrained contexts (1-2 weeks available)
- Sufficient existing research (focused question)
- Stakeholder needs rapid evidence

**❌ Skip systematic approach when**:
- Question too broad or undefined
- Very little research exists (<5 relevant papers)
- Purely theoretical question
- Narrative review more appropriate
- Resources insufficient for quality work

## Recent Developments (2024-2025)

### Living Systematic Reviews

**Definition**: Continually updated systematic reviews that incorporate new evidence as it becomes available

**Purpose**: Keep reviews current in rapidly evolving fields

**Challenges**: Resource intensive, requires infrastructure

**Source**: Cochrane guidance on living systematic reviews

---

### AI-Assisted Screening

**Tools**: ASReview, Rayyan AI, Cochrane Crowd

**Purpose**: Use machine learning to prioritize likely relevant papers in screening

**Evidence**: Can reduce screening burden by 30-50% while maintaining recall

**Caution**: Human validation still required; report tool use in PRISMA #7

---

### Automated Data Extraction

**Tools**: RobotReviewer, DistillerSR automation

**Purpose**: Automated extraction of study characteristics and outcomes

**Status**: Emerging technology, not yet validated for unsupervised use

**Best practice**: Use as assistance, always validate manually
