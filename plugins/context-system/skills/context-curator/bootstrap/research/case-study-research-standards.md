# Case Study Research Standards

**Purpose**: Best practices for case study research methodology based on Robert Yin's framework

**Created**: 2026-02-08
**Status**: Research synthesis for `/researcher` skill implementation

---

## Overview

Case study research involves in-depth investigation of a contemporary phenomenon within its real-world context, especially when boundaries between phenomenon and context are not clearly evident.

**Core principle**: Multiple sources of evidence with systematic data collection and analysis.

---

## Yin's Case Study Framework

### Three Types of Case Studies

1. **Descriptive**: Describe a phenomenon in its context
   - **Purpose**: Document what exists
   - **Example**: Describing how a data team operates

2. **Explanatory**: Explain how or why something occurred
   - **Purpose**: Understand causal mechanisms
   - **Example**: Why did data platform migration succeed/fail?

3. **Exploratory**: Identify research questions or procedures for future study
   - **Purpose**: Preliminary investigation
   - **Example**: Explore emerging AI adoption patterns

**Source**: [Case Study Research: Design and Methods - Robert Yin](https://us.sagepub.com/en-us/nam/case-study-research-and-applications/book250150)

---

## Key Characteristics of Yin's Approach

### Methodological Rigor

**Core elements**:
- **Multiple sources of evidence**: Documents, interviews, observations, archival records
- **Theoretical propositions**: Guide data collection and analysis
- **Pattern matching**: Compare empirical patterns with predicted patterns
- **Explanation building**: Iterative process of causal explanation
- **Time-series analysis**: Track changes over time

**Why rigor matters**: Addresses critiques that case studies lack scientific rigor.

**Source**: [Robert K. Yin Case Study Approaches](https://jeisilaguilar.com/case-study-approaches-stake-1998-simons-2011-and-yin-2014/)

---

### Research Design Components

**Five components of case study design**:
1. **Research questions**: "How" and "why" questions (case studies excel here)
2. **Propositions/hypotheses**: What to study (theoretical guidance)
3. **Unit of analysis**: What is the "case"? (individual, project, organization)
4. **Logic linking data to propositions**: How will data address questions?
5. **Criteria for interpreting findings**: How to draw conclusions?

**Critical**: Define these upfront before data collection.

**Source**: [Case Study Research: Design and Methods - Yin](https://www.amazon.com/Case-Study-Research-Applications-Methods/dp/1506336167)

## Single vs. Multiple Case Studies

### Single Case Design

**When to use**:
- Critical case (tests well-formulated theory)
- Unique or extreme case
- Representative or typical case
- Revelatory case (previously inaccessible)
- Longitudinal case (study over time)

**Strength**: Depth of analysis
**Weakness**: Limited generalizability

---

### Multiple Case Design

**When to use**:
- Want replication logic (literal or theoretical)
- Strengthen evidence through cross-case patterns
- Compare different contexts or outcomes

**Strength**: More robust conclusions
**Weakness**: Requires more resources

**Replication logic**: Each case is a separate "experiment" - finding same pattern across cases strengthens conclusions.

**Source**: [Yin Case Study Methodology](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/308385754_Robert_K_Yin_2014_Case_Study_Research_Design_and_Methods_5th_ed_Thousand_Oaks_CA_Sage_282_pages)

## Data Collection Methods

### Six Sources of Evidence

1. **Documentation**: Memos, reports, emails, meeting notes
2. **Archival records**: Organizational charts, financial data, survey data
3. **Interviews**: Structured, semi-structured, or unstructured
4. **Direct observations**: Field visits, observing meetings
5. **Participant observation**: Researcher actively participates
6. **Physical artifacts**: Technology, tools, workspaces

**Principle**: Use multiple sources to triangulate findings.

**Source**: [Case Study Research - Yin](https://books.google.com/books/about/Case_Study_Research.html?id=BWea_9ZGQMwC)

## Analysis Techniques

### Pattern Matching

**Process**: Compare empirically observed pattern with predicted pattern.

**Types**:
- **Nonequivalent dependent variables**: Multiple predicted outcomes
- **Rival explanations**: Compare to alternative theories
- **Simpler patterns**: Categorical or descriptive patterns

**When patterns match**: Theory supported
**When patterns don't match**: Theory questioned, alternative explanations explored

---

### Explanation Building

**Purpose**: Build causal explanation through iterative refinement.

**Process**:
1. State initial proposition
2. Compare with evidence from first case
3. Revise proposition
4. Compare with evidence from additional cases
5. Repeat until stable explanation emerges

**Caution**: Avoid drift from original research questions.

---

### Time-Series Analysis

**Purpose**: Track changes over time to identify trends or effects.

**Types**:
- **Simple time series**: Track single variable
- **Chronology**: Sequence of events
- **Complex time series**: Multiple variables and relationships

**Use**: Especially powerful for process studies and longitudinal cases.

**Source**: [Yin's Analytical Techniques](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/308385754_Robert_K_Yin_2014_Case_Study_Research_Design_and_Methods_5th_ed_Thousand_Oaks_CA_Sage_282_pages)

## Recent Updates (Latest Editions)

### Emphasis on Rival Explanations

**Enhancement**: Greater emphasis on using plausible rival explanations to bolster case study quality.

**Why**: Considering alternatives strengthens conclusions by ruling out competing explanations.

**How**: Explicitly identify rival explanations, gather data to test them, compare support.

---

### Synthesizing Across Cases

**Enhancement**: More detailed guidance on synthesizing findings across multiple cases in multiple-case studies.

**Techniques**:
- Cross-case synthesis tables
- Word tables displaying case data
- Pattern-matching across cases
- Explanation building across cases

**Source**: [Case Study Research and Applications - Recent Edition](https://uk.sagepub.com/en-gb/eur/case-study-research-and-applications/book250150)

## Validity and Reliability

### Four Quality Tests

1. **Construct validity**: Use multiple sources, establish chain of evidence, have key informants review draft
2. **Internal validity**: Pattern matching, explanation building, rival explanations, logic models
3. **External validity**: Replication logic in multiple-case studies, theoretical generalization
4. **Reliability**: Case study protocol, case study database, document procedures

**Critical**: Address these throughout study, not just at end.

**Source**: [Designing Research with Case Study Methods - Sage](https://researchmethodscommunity.sagepub.com/blog/designing-research-with-case-study-methods)

---

## Case Study Protocol

### Protocol Components

**Essential sections**:
1. **Overview**: Background, research questions, theoretical framework
2. **Data collection procedures**: Sites, access, sources, schedule
3. **Questions**: Specific questions to address (guide for researcher)
4. **Guide for report**: Outline, format, audience

**Why protocols matter**: Increase reliability, guide data collection, enable replication.

**Best practice**: Always create protocol before data collection.

---

## Timeline

**Typical case study timeline**:
- **Planning & protocol**: 1 week
- **Data collection**: 2-4 weeks (per case)
- **Analysis**: 1-2 weeks (per case)
- **Cross-case synthesis**: 1-2 weeks (if multiple cases)
- **Writing & validation**: 1-2 weeks

**Total**: 2-6 weeks for single case, 4-12 weeks for multiple cases

## When to Use Case Studies

**✅ Use when**:
- Research question is "how" or "why"
- Cannot manipulate behavior (not experiment)
- Want to understand context deeply
- Boundaries between phenomenon and context unclear

**❌ Skip when**:
- Need statistical generalization (use survey)
- Want to control variables (use experiment)
- Phenomenon is not contemporary (use historical analysis)
