# Citation Network Mapping

**Purpose**: Track theoretical lineage, identify foundational works, and understand schools of thought in your research domain.

**When to use**: After synthesizing findings, to understand the intellectual structure of the field.

---

## Why Citation Mapping Matters

### Academic Context
- Identifies foundational papers everyone builds on
- Shows theoretical lineage and evolution
- Reveals competing schools of thought
- Helps identify seminal works to prioritize
- Demonstrates understanding of field structure

### Practical Benefits
- Know which papers are most influential
- Understand theoretical debates
- Identify key research groups
- Find additional papers through citation trails
- Strengthen your own literature review credibility

---

## Citation Network Structure

### Three Tiers of Papers

**Tier 1: Foundational Works** (Pre-2010 or seminal)
- Highly cited across reviewed papers
- Established core concepts/theories
- Everyone references these
- May be outside your time window but still essential

**Tier 2: Contemporary Leaders** (2010-2020)
- Built on foundations
- High citation count in recent work
- Shaped current thinking
- Bridge between classic and current

**Tier 3: Recent Developments** (2020+)
- Latest research
- May not be highly cited yet
- Represents cutting edge
- Shows where field is heading

## How to Build Citation Network

### Step 1: Count Citations
As you read papers, track:
- Which older papers are frequently cited?
- Which recent papers cite each other?
- Who cites whom?

Create simple tally:
```
Ebbinghaus (1885): |||||||||| (10 citations)
Bjork & Bjork (1992): |||||||| (8 citations)
Dunlosky (2013): |||||| (6 citations)
```

### Step 2: Identify Theoretical Families
Group papers by:
- Shared theoretical framework
- Common methodology
- Related research questions
- Author networks (same research groups)

### Step 3: Track Evolution
Map how ideas evolved:
- Early hypothesis → Testing → Refinement → Current understanding
- Original concept → Extensions → Modifications → Critiques

### Step 4: Note Competing Views
Where do schools of thought diverge?
- Different assumptions
- Different methods
- Different interpretations of same data
- Unresolved debates

## Example: Spaced Repetition Citation Network

```markdown
## Citation Network Example

### Foundational Papers (Pre-2010)

**Ebbinghaus (1885): Memory: A Contribution to Experimental Psychology**
- **Why Foundational**: Discovered forgetting curve, established experimental memory research
- **Citation Frequency**: Cited by 89% of reviewed papers
- **Key Contribution**: First empirical study of memory retention over time
- **Lineage**: All modern spacing effect research traces to this work

**Bjork & Bjork (1992): A New Theory of Disuse**
- **Why Foundational**: "Desirable difficulties" framework
- **Citation Frequency**: Cited by 67% of reviewed papers
- **Key Contribution**: Explained why harder retrieval improves retention
- **Lineage**: Influenced all contemporary work on retrieval practice

**Cepeda et al. (2006): Distributed Practice in Verbal Recall Tasks**
- **Why Foundational**: Largest meta-analysis of spacing effect
- **Citation Frequency**: Cited by 78% of reviewed papers
- **Key Contribution**: 317 studies, established robust effect
- **Lineage**: Set standard for spacing effect research

---

### Contemporary Leaders (High Citation Count)

**Dunlosky et al. (2013): Improving Students' Learning**
- **Citation Count**: Cited by 45% of papers (2015-2026)
- **Contribution**: Compared 10 learning techniques, ranked spaced practice #1
- **Influenced**: Shifted education research toward evidence-based study strategies

**Kang (2016): Spaced Repetition Promotes Efficient Learning**
- **Citation Count**: Cited by 34% of recent papers
- **Contribution**: Neurological evidence for spacing effects
- **Influenced**: Integration of cognitive neuroscience into spacing research

---

### Theoretical Schools

**School 1: Cognitive Load Theory**
- **Core Premise**: Spacing reduces cognitive load, allows better encoding
- **Key Figures**: Sweller, Paas, van Merriënboer
- **Focus**: Optimal cognitive load for learning
- **Strengths**: Explains why massed practice overloads working memory

**School 2: Desirable Difficulties**
- **Core Premise**: Retrieval challenge strengthens memory traces
- **Key Figures**: Bjork, Bjork, Kornell
- **Focus**: Difficulty of retrieval determines learning
- **Strengths**: Explains counter-intuitive finding that harder = better

**Integration**: Recent work combines both perspectives—spacing both reduces load during encoding AND increases retrieval difficulty during practice.
```

## Analyzing Your Citation Network

### Questions to Ask

**About Foundational Works**:
- What are the 3-5 papers everyone cites?
- What core concepts came from these papers?
- Are these papers still valid, or have they been challenged?

**About Theoretical Lineage**:
- How has thinking evolved over time?
- What early assumptions have been revised?
- What new questions have emerged?

**About Research Groups**:
- Are there identifiable research groups/labs?
- Do certain authors consistently work together?
- Are there competing labs with different approaches?

**About Current State**:
- What's the frontier of research now?
- Where is the field headed?
- What recent papers are gaining influence?

---

## Integration with Literature Review

Include citation network analysis in your review:

**In Introduction**:
- Establish theoretical foundation
- Show awareness of seminal works

**In Methodology**:
- Explain theoretical framework used
- Justify methodological choices with citations

**In Findings**:
- Reference theoretical schools when discussing different perspectives
- Show how your synthesis fits into intellectual lineage

**In Discussion**:
- Connect to foundational concepts
- Show how findings advance or challenge existing theory
