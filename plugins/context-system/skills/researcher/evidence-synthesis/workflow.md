# Evidence Synthesis & Systematic Review Workflow

**Purpose**: Conduct rigorous synthesis of academic research literature using PRISMA 2020 methodology with protocol registration, quality assessment, and publication-ready reporting.

**Status**: Production

## Overview

Evidence synthesis systematically identifies, evaluates, and synthesizes academic research to answer specific questions, identify patterns, and reveal gaps in the literature.

**Core principle**: Systematic and transparent methodology to minimize bias and ensure reproducibility.

**When to use**: Academic research, thesis/dissertation, evidence-based policy, understanding research landscape, identifying research gaps.

## What is Evidence Synthesis?

### Core Concept

**Evidence synthesis** = Systematic identification, evaluation, and synthesis of research literature

**Not just**: Literature review (narrative summary)
**But rather**: Rigorous methodology following established standards (PRISMA 2020)

**Key differences**:

| Narrative Review | Systematic Review |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Informal search | Documented search strategy |
| Subjective selection | Pre-defined inclusion criteria |
| Descriptive summary | Structured data extraction |
| No quality assessment | Quality assessment required |
| Author interpretation | Transparent synthesis |

---

### Two Variants

**1. Full Systematic Review** (4-8 weeks)
- Comprehensive search across databases
- Protocol registration (PROSPERO)
- Duplicate screening and extraction
- Quality assessment (AMSTAR-2, ROBIS)
- Full PRISMA 2020 compliance
- **Use when**: Academic publication, high-stakes decision, need external validity

**2. Rapid Evidence Assessment (REA)** (1-2 weeks)
- Focused search (key databases only)
- Streamlined protocol (no registration)
- Single reviewer (with quality checks)
- Abbreviated quality assessment
- PRISMA-inspired but pragmatic
- **Use when**: Time-constrained, exploratory, internal decision-making

**See**: [[rea-approach]] for REA methodology

---

### PRISMA 2020 Framework

**PRISMA** = Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses

**27-item checklist** ensuring:
- Transparent reporting
- Replicable methods
- Minimized bias
- Quality assessment

## Six-Phase Workflow (Full Systematic Review)

### Timeline

**Full systematic review**: 4-8 weeks

**Phase breakdown**:
- Phase 0: Protocol Development (2-4 days + 1-3 weeks PROSPERO approval)
- Phase 1: Scoping & Search (2-3 days)
- Phase 2: Paper Processing (1-3 weeks, 15-30 min per paper)
- Phase 3: Cross-Paper Synthesis (3-5 days)
- Phase 4: Gap Analysis (1-2 days)
- Phase 5: Reporting (2-3 days)

---

## Phase 0: Protocol Development (PRISMA-P)

**Goal**: Create and register review protocol before starting.

**Duration**: 2-4 hours protocol creation + 1-3 weeks PROSPERO approval

**Why protocol matters**:
- Prevents post-hoc modifications (p-hacking)
- Ensures transparency (public record)
- Avoids duplication (others can see review in progress)
- Required by academic journals

---

### Activities

**1. Define research question using PICO** (1-2 hours):

**PICO framework**:
- **P**opulation: Who/what is studied?
- **I**ntervention/Exposure: What is being examined?
- **C**omparator: Compared to what?
- **O**utcome: What is measured?

**Example**:
- **P**: Adult learners
- **I**: Spaced repetition
- **C**: Massed practice
- **O**: Long-term retention

**Research question**: "How effective is spaced repetition compared to massed practice for long-term knowledge retention in adult learners?"

---

**2. Establish inclusion/exclusion criteria** (1 hour):

**Inclusion**:
- Study types (RCTs, observational, qualitative)
- Time period (e.g., 2010-2025)
- Language (English, or multilingual)
- Publication type (peer-reviewed only)

**Exclusion**:
- Opinion pieces without data
- Duplicate publications
- Irrelevant populations or outcomes

---

**3. Document search strategy** (1-2 hours):

**Databases to search**:
- Academic: PubMed, PsycINFO, Scopus, Web of Science
- Preprints: arXiv, bioRxiv, SSRN
- Grey literature: Google Scholar, OpenGrey

**Search terms**:
- Primary terms (e.g., "spaced repetition", "distributed practice")
- Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT)
- Synonyms and variations

**Example search string**:
```
("spaced repetition" OR "distributed practice") AND
("retention" OR "memory" OR "learning") AND
("adult*" OR "university" OR "professional")
```

---

**4. Specify quality assessment methods** (30 min):

**For systematic reviews**:
- AMSTAR-2 (quality of systematic reviews)
- ROBIS (risk of bias in systematic reviews)

**For primary studies**:
- Cochrane Risk of Bias tool (RCTs)
- Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (observational studies)
- CASP checklists (qualitative studies)

---

**5. Pre-specify synthesis approach** (30 min):

**Synthesis types**:
- **Thematic synthesis**: Organize findings by theme
- **Meta-analysis**: Quantitative pooling (if studies comparable)
- **Narrative synthesis**: Structured narrative with tables

**Subgroup analyses** (if planned):
- By population (e.g., age groups)
- By study design (e.g., RCT vs observational)
- By intervention characteristics (e.g., spacing intervals)

---

**6. Register protocol on PROSPERO** (1 hour submission + 1-3 weeks approval):

**PROSPERO** = International register of systematic review protocols

**Submission**:
- Complete PRISMA-P 17-item checklist
- Submit to [PROSPERO](https://www.crd.york.ac.uk/prospero/)
- Receive registration number
- Cite in final review

**Note**: PROSPERO is free but review takes 1-3 weeks. For internal/time-sensitive reviews, registration is optional (but document protocol internally).

---

### Phase 0 Outputs

- ✅ Research question (PICO format)
- ✅ Inclusion/exclusion criteria
- ✅ Search strategy documented
- ✅ Quality assessment methods specified
- ✅ Synthesis approach pre-specified
- ✅ Protocol registered (PROSPERO number)

**See**: [[protocol-phase]] for detailed PRISMA-P checklist and templates

## Phase 1: Scoping & Search

**Goal**: Identify papers that meet inclusion criteria.

**Duration**: 2-3 days

---

### Activities

**1. Execute search strategy** (1 day):
- Run searches in all planned databases
- Record results per database (for PRISMA flow diagram)
- Export citations to reference manager (Zotero, Mendeley, EndNote)

**Example results**:
- PubMed: 245 results
- PsycINFO: 189 results
- Scopus: 312 results
- Total: 746 results

---

**2. Remove duplicates** (2-3 hours):
- Use reference manager deduplication
- Manual check for near-duplicates (different DOIs, same paper)

**Example**: 746 → 523 unique records

---

**3. Screen titles and abstracts** (1-2 days):
- Review each title/abstract against inclusion criteria
- Classify: Include, Exclude, or Maybe
- Duplicate screening (2+ reviewers) for rigor (full systematic review)
- Resolve disagreements through discussion

**Example**: 523 screened → 67 potentially eligible

---

**4. Full-text screening** (2-3 hours):
- Retrieve full-text PDFs for potentially eligible papers
- Review full text against inclusion criteria
- Document exclusion reasons

**Example**: 67 full-text reviewed → 24 included in final review

---

**5. Create PRISMA flow diagram** (30 min):

**Standard format**:
```
Records identified through database search (n=746)
    ↓
Records after duplicates removed (n=523)
    ↓
Records screened (title/abstract) (n=523) → Excluded (n=456)
    ↓
Full-text articles assessed (n=67) → Excluded (n=43)
    ↓
Studies included in review (n=24)
```

**See**: [[prisma-compliance]] for full PRISMA flow diagram template

---

### Phase 1 Outputs

- ✅ Search results documented (per database)
- ✅ Duplicates removed
- ✅ Screening completed (titles/abstracts, full-text)
- ✅ Final included papers identified (n=X)
- ✅ PRISMA flow diagram

---

## Phase 2: Paper Processing

**Goal**: Extract systematic data from each included paper.

**Duration**: 1-3 weeks (15-30 min per paper × number of papers)

---

### Activities

**1. Create extraction template** (1 hour):

**Standard fields**:
- **Metadata**: Authors, year, journal, DOI
- **Study design**: Method, sample size, setting
- **Population**: Demographics, characteristics
- **Intervention/Exposure**: What was studied
- **Outcomes**: What was measured, effect sizes
- **Findings**: Key results
- **Quality**: Strengths, limitations, bias assessment
- **Theoretical framework**: Underlying theory (if applicable)

---

**2. Extract data systematically** (15-30 min per paper):

**Process**:
- Read full paper
- Complete extraction template
- Save as individual file per paper

**Example filename**: `author-year-title.md`

**Duplicate extraction** (optional, for rigor):
- 2+ reviewers extract independently
- Compare, resolve disagreements

---

**3. Assess study quality** (10-15 min per paper):

**Use appropriate tool**:
- RCTs: Cochrane Risk of Bias tool
- Observational: Newcastle-Ottawa Scale
- Systematic reviews: AMSTAR-2

**Document**:
- Quality rating (high/moderate/low)
- Key quality concerns
- Impact on confidence in findings

---

### Phase 2 Outputs

- ✅ Extraction template created
- ✅ Data extracted from all papers (n=X files)
- ✅ Quality assessment completed per paper

**See**: [[paper-processing]] for detailed extraction template and best practices

## Phase 3: Cross-Paper Synthesis

**Goal**: Synthesize findings thematically across papers.

**Duration**: 3-5 days

**Critical principle**: Organize by theme, NOT paper-by-paper.

---

### Activities

**1. Create methodology comparison table** (1 day):

**Table format**:
| Study | Design | Sample | Intervention | Outcome | Effect Size | Quality |
|-------|--------|--------|--------------|---------|-------------|---------|
| Author1 (Year) | RCT | n=120 | Spaced (1d, 7d) | Retention | d=0.65 | High |
| Author2 (Year) | Quasi | n=85 | Spaced (varied) | Recall | r=.42 | Moderate |

**Purpose**: Enable comparison of methods, samples, effects

---

**2. Thematic synthesis** (2-3 days):

**Process**:
1. Read all papers, identify recurring themes
2. Code findings by theme (not by paper)
3. Organize themes hierarchically
4. Write narrative synthesis per theme with evidence

**Example themes**:
- **Theme 1: Optimal spacing intervals** (12/24 papers)
  - Evidence: Author1 (2020) found 1-7 day spacing optimal for retention (d=0.65)
  - Evidence: Author5 (2022) replicated with 2-10 day spacing (d=0.58)
  - Consensus: Spacing intervals of 1-10 days show consistent benefit

- **Theme 2: Individual differences** (8/24 papers)
  - Evidence: Author3 (2021) found age moderates effect (stronger for younger adults)
  - Debate: Author9 (2023) found no age difference in large sample

**Not this** (paper-by-paper summary):
- ❌ "Author1 studied spaced repetition and found..."
- ❌ "Author2 then examined..."

**But this** (thematic synthesis):
- ✅ "Optimal spacing intervals vary from 1-10 days (12 studies, n=2,400)..."
- ✅ "Individual differences may moderate effects (8 studies), though findings are mixed..."

---

**3. Map citation networks** (1-2 days):

**Identify**:
- **Foundational papers** (highly cited by others in your sample)
- **Recent developments** (past 2-3 years)
- **Theoretical lineage** (who built on whom)
- **Competing schools** (different theoretical approaches)

**Example**:
- Ebbinghaus (1885) → foundational work on spacing
- Cepeda et al. (2006) → major meta-analysis (cited by 18/24 papers)
- Bjork & Bjork (2011) → "desirable difficulties" framework (cited by 15/24)
- Recent: Chen et al. (2024) → neural mechanisms (cited by 3 recent papers)

---

### Phase 3 Outputs

- ✅ Methodology comparison table
- ✅ Thematic synthesis (3-7 major themes)
- ✅ Citation network map (foundational + recent)

**See**:
- [[methodology-comparison]] for synthesis techniques
- [[citation-mapping]] for network analysis

## Phase 4: Gap Analysis

**Goal**: Identify what's missing in current research.

**Duration**: 1-2 days

---

### Five Types of Gaps

**1. Methodological gaps**:
- **What's missing**: Few longitudinal studies, most are lab-based
- **Recommendation**: Conduct real-world longitudinal study of spaced practice

**2. Population gaps**:
- **What's missing**: Elderly learners understudied (only 2/24 papers)
- **Recommendation**: Examine spacing effects in older adults (age 65+)

**3. Content gaps**:
- **What's missing**: Most studies focus on factual recall, few on skill acquisition
- **Recommendation**: Test spaced practice for procedural learning

**4. Theoretical gaps**:
- **What's missing**: Mechanisms unclear (neural basis, consolidation)
- **Recommendation**: Neuroscience studies to understand underlying processes

**5. Contextual gaps**:
- **What's missing**: Most studies in education, few in professional training
- **Recommendation**: Apply to workplace learning contexts

---

### Phase 4 Outputs

- ✅ Gap analysis (5 gap types identified)
- ✅ Research recommendations (specific future studies)

**See**: [[gap-analysis]] for detailed templates and examples

## Phase 5: Reporting

**Goal**: Generate publication-ready systematic review document.

**Duration**: 2-3 days

---

### Report Structure (PRISMA 2020 compliant)

**Systematic review report** (15-30 pages):

1. **Title**: "A Systematic Review of [Topic]"

2. **Abstract** (150-250 words)
   - Background, methods, results, conclusions
   - Structured format (PRISMA abstract checklist)

3. **Introduction** (2-3 pages)
   - Rationale for review
   - Research question (PICO)
   - Objectives

4. **Methods** (3-4 pages)
   - Protocol registration (PROSPERO number)
   - Eligibility criteria
   - Search strategy (databases, terms)
   - Screening process (duplicate reviewers)
   - Data extraction
   - Quality assessment
   - Synthesis methods

5. **Results** (8-12 pages)
   - Study selection (PRISMA flow diagram)
   - Study characteristics (methodology table)
   - Quality assessment summary
   - **Synthesized findings** (thematic, NOT paper-by-paper)

6. **Discussion** (3-5 pages)
   - Summary of evidence
   - Comparison to prior reviews
   - Strengths and limitations
   - Implications for practice/policy/research

7. **Conclusions** (1-2 pages)
   - Answer to research question
   - Research gaps
   - Future directions

8. **References** (all papers + methods sources)

9. **Appendices**
   - Search strategies (full strings per database)
   - Extraction template
   - PRISMA 2020 checklist (27 items completed)
   - Quality assessment details

## Core Principles

### 1. Academic Rigor

**Requirements**:
- Proper citation with author names and years
- Methodology comparison across studies
- Clear distinction between findings and interpretation
- Identification of theoretical frameworks
- Research gaps and future directions

---

### 2. Systematic Rather Than Narrative

**Follow PRISMA 2020**:
- Create protocol before starting (PRISMA-P)
- Register protocol (PROSPERO) for full reviews
- Define research question (PICO framework)
- Establish inclusion/exclusion criteria
- Document comprehensive search strategy
- Extract data systematically (ideally duplicate reviewers)
- Assess risk of bias using established tools
- Synthesize findings thematically
- Report transparently (PRISMA 2020 checklist)

---

### 3. Critical Analysis Over Summary

**Don't just summarize** - analyze:
- Methodological strengths and limitations
- Contradicting findings (explore why)
- Evolution of thought over time
- Gaps in current research

---

### 4. Citation Network Awareness

**Track who cites whom**:
- Foundational papers (highly cited)
- Recent developments
- Theoretical lineage
- Competing schools of thought

## Troubleshooting

### "Too many papers (hundreds), can't process all"

**Symptom**: Search returns 500+ papers, full extraction infeasible

**Solutions**:
1. **Refine inclusion criteria**: Be more specific (e.g., add population restriction)
2. **Focus on high-quality studies**: Exclude low-quality journals or non-peer-reviewed
3. **Sample strategically**: Include all recent papers (past 5 years) + highly cited older papers
4. **Consider REA**: Switch to Rapid Evidence Assessment (see [[rea-approach]])

---

### "Can't access paywalled papers"

**Symptom**: Many papers behind paywalls, can't retrieve full text

**Solutions**:
1. **Institutional access**: Use university library credentials
2. **Inter-library loan**: Request through library
3. **Open access repositories**: Check author's website, ResearchGate, arXiv, PubMed Central
4. **Contact authors**: Email authors directly (often happy to share)
5. **Exclusion justification**: Document exclusions due to unavailability (PRISMA allows this)

---

### "No quality assessment tool fits my studies"

**Symptom**: Mixed study designs (RCTs + qualitative + case studies)

**Solutions**:
1. **Use multiple tools**: Different tool per study type
2. **Mixed Methods Appraisal Tool (MMAT)**: Designed for mixed reviews
3. **Custom criteria**: Develop criteria based on review needs (document rationale)

---

### "Findings are contradictory across studies"

**Symptom**: Some studies find effect, others don't

**Solutions**:
1. **Don't force consensus**: Report divergence honestly
2. **Explore moderators**: Do study characteristics explain differences? (sample size, methods, context)
3. **Meta-analysis**: Quantify heterogeneity (I² statistic)
4. **Narrative synthesis**: "Findings are mixed, with X showing positive effects and Y showing null effects. Differences may be due to..."

## Known Limitations

**Evidence synthesis may not be appropriate when**:
- Insufficient research exists (<5 papers)
- Research question too broad (narrow it first)
- Rapid turnaround needed (<1 week) - consider targeted search instead
- Non-academic sources primary (use general research synthesis instead)

**Evidence synthesis requires**:
- Access to academic databases
- Time (1-2 weeks minimum for REA, 4-8 weeks for full review)
- Research skills (critical appraisal, synthesis)
- Acceptance that some gaps exist (not all questions have research answers)

**Evidence synthesis excels when**:
- Need comprehensive understanding of academic literature
- Informing evidence-based policy or practice
- Identifying research gaps
- Academic publication or thesis
