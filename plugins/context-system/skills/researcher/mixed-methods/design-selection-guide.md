# Mixed Methods Design Selection Guide

**Purpose**: Detailed decision criteria for choosing the right mixed methods design.

**Created**: 2026-02-08

---

## Design Overview

Three core designs (Creswell & Plano Clark):

1. **Convergent Design** - Collect qual + quant simultaneously, merge findings
2. **Explanatory Sequential** - Quant first → qual explains results
3. **Exploratory Sequential** - Qual first → quant tests insights

**This guide helps you choose** based on research question, timing, priority, and resources.

---

## Decision Framework

### Step 1: Timing Assessment

**Question**: Can you collect data simultaneously or must it be sequential?

**Simultaneous** → Consider Convergent Design
- You can run surveys and interviews in parallel
- Resources allow dual data collection
- Findings don't need to inform each other during collection

**Sequential** → Consider Sequential Designs (go to Step 2)
- One method must inform the other
- Resource constraints (can only do one at a time)
- Learning from Phase 1 is critical for Phase 2 design

---

### Step 2: Priority Assessment (If Sequential)

**Question**: Which method has higher priority?

**Quantitative priority** → Explanatory Sequential Design
- Your main question is quantitative (how many, how much, what predicts)
- Qualitative is supplemental (explains, provides depth)
- Example: "What factors predict employee turnover (quant priority), and why do people leave (qual supplement)?"

**Qualitative priority** → Exploratory Sequential Design
- Your main question is qualitative (understand phenomenon)
- Quantitative is supplemental (tests prevalence, validates)
- Example: "What motivates knowledge sharing (qual priority), and how prevalent are these motivations (quant supplement)?"

**Equal priority** → Reconsider Convergent Design
- If both methods are equally important, why sequential?
- Unless one must inform the other (development rationale)

---

### Step 3: Purpose Assessment

**Question**: Why are you mixing methods?

**Four rationales** (Greene et al.):

**1. Triangulation** (Validate findings)
- Purpose: Corroborate findings across methods
- Best design: **Convergent** (collect both, compare)
- Example: Do survey results align with interview themes?

**2. Complementarity** (Elaborate findings)
- Purpose: Elaborate, enhance, clarify results from one method
- Best design: **Explanatory Sequential** (quant → qual explains)
- Example: Survey shows satisfaction scores, interviews reveal why

**3. Development** (Inform instruments)
- Purpose: Use one method to develop the other
- Best design: **Exploratory Sequential** (qual → quant instrument)
- Example: Interviews identify constructs → survey measures constructs

**4. Expansion** (Extend scope)
- Purpose: Extend breadth and range of inquiry
- Best design: **Convergent** (different aspects simultaneously)
- Example: Quant measures outcomes, qual explores process

## Design Profiles

### Convergent Design (Parallel)

**Structure**:
```
Qual Data Collection  ────┐
                          ├──→ Merge Results → Integrated Interpretation
Quant Data Collection ────┘
```

**When to use**:
- ✅ Want comprehensive view from multiple angles
- ✅ Can collect data simultaneously
- ✅ Resources allow parallel collection
- ✅ Equal priority to both methods
- ✅ Want to triangulate or expand findings

**When NOT to use**:
- ❌ One method must inform the other
- ❌ Resource constraints (can only do one at a time)
- ❌ Clear priority to one method
- ❌ Instruments need development from initial data

**Strengths**:
- Efficient (parallel collection)
- Comprehensive (multiple perspectives)
- Triangulation validates findings
- Equal weight to qual and quant

**Challenges**:
- Complex integration (different data types)
- Contradictions require resolution
- Need skills in both methods simultaneously
- Larger sample needed for quant

**Example research questions**:
- "What is the prevalence of burnout (quant) and what are the lived experiences of burnout (qual)?"
- "How do teams perform on collaboration metrics (quant) and what collaboration practices do they use (qual)?"
- "What percentage of users adopt new features (quant) and why do they adopt or not (qual)?"

**Integration strategy**: Create joint display comparing qual themes with quant statistics

---

### Explanatory Sequential Design (QUAN → qual)

**Structure**:
```
Quant Data Collection → Quant Analysis → Identify Results to Explain
                                              ↓
                                    Qual Data Collection
                                              ↓
                                    Integrated Interpretation
```

**When to use**:
- ✅ Primary question is quantitative
- ✅ Quant results need explanation or depth
- ✅ Unexpected or unclear quant findings
- ✅ Want to understand mechanisms behind patterns
- ✅ Follow-up on interesting quant results

**When NOT to use**:
- ❌ Primary question is qualitative
- ❌ Need to develop instruments (use exploratory instead)
- ❌ Quant findings are straightforward (no need to explain)
- ❌ Can't wait for quant results before qual design

**Strengths**:
- Clear sequence (less complex coordination)
- Qual sample can be targeted (based on quant results)
- Efficient qual data collection (focused questions)
- Provides depth to quant findings

**Challenges**:
- Time-intensive (sequential)
- Quant results must be analyzed before qual starts
- Risk: Qual may not fully explain quant (need contingency)
- Participant fatigue if same sample

**Example research questions**:
- "Which factors predict customer churn (quant), and why do customers leave (qual)?"
- "How does training affect performance (quant), and what mechanisms explain the effect (qual)?"
- "What demographics correlate with adoption (quant), and what barriers do non-adopters face (qual)?"

**Integration strategy**: Quant identifies patterns → qual explains why patterns exist

**Variants**:

**Participant selection variant**:
- Use quant to identify typical, extreme, or contrasting cases
- Interview selected cases in qual phase
- Example: Survey all employees, interview high vs low performers

**Follow-up explanations variant**:
- Use quant to identify surprising or unclear results
- Explore those specific results in qual phase
- Example: Survey shows unexpected spike in complaints, interviews explore why

---

### Exploratory Sequential Design (QUAL → quan)

**Structure**:
```
Qual Data Collection → Qual Analysis → Extract Themes/Constructs
                                              ↓
                                    Develop Quant Instrument
                                              ↓
                                    Quant Data Collection
                                              ↓
                                    Integrated Interpretation
```

**When to use**:
- ✅ Primary question is qualitative
- ✅ Need to develop measurement instrument
- ✅ New or poorly understood phenomenon
- ✅ Want to generalize qual findings to population
- ✅ Testing emergent theory

**When NOT to use**:
- ❌ Primary question is quantitative
- ❌ Validated instruments already exist
- ❌ Phenomenon is well-understood
- ❌ Timeline too short for sequential design

**Strengths**:
- Grounded instrument (based on participant language)
- Theory-driven quantitative phase
- Ensures quant measures what matters (ecological validity)
- Generalizes qual insights

**Challenges**:
- Most time-intensive design (two full phases)
- Instrument development requires psychometric expertise
- Risk: Quant may not validate qual themes
- Complex: Requires qual → quant transformation

**Example research questions**:
- "What factors motivate open source contributions (qual), and how prevalent are these factors (quan)?"
- "What dimensions define organizational culture (qual), and how do organizations differ on these dimensions (quan)?"
- "What barriers prevent adoption (qual), and which barriers are most common (quan)?"

**Integration strategy**: Qual themes → quant items → validate relationships at scale

**Variants**:

**Instrument development variant**:
- Qual: Identify constructs and generate items
- Quant: Validate scale (factor analysis, reliability)
- Example: Interview 20 to develop motivation scale, survey 300 to validate

**Taxonomy development variant**:
- Qual: Develop classification or typology
- Quant: Test taxonomy's validity and coverage
- Example: Interview to identify user types, survey to confirm types exist

**Theory testing variant**:
- Qual: Generate hypotheses or theoretical model
- Quant: Test hypotheses or model fit
- Example: Case studies suggest X causes Y, survey tests causality

## Advanced Design Considerations

### Sample Size Guidelines

**Qualitative**:
- Exploratory: 5-10 (initial understanding)
- Saturation: 15-25 (comprehensive themes)
- Comparative: 10-20 per group (cross-case patterns)

**Quantitative**:
- Descriptive: 100-300 (stable estimates)
- Correlational: 200-500 (detect medium effects)
- Experimental: Power analysis (typically 30+ per group)

**Mixed methods**:
- Convergent: Often different samples (e.g., n=500 quant, n=20 qual)
- Explanatory: Qual sample subset of quant (e.g., interview 15 from 300 survey)
- Exploratory: Qual sample small, quant sample large (e.g., 10 → 300)

---

### Sampling Strategy

**Four approaches**:

**1. Identical sampling** (same participants)
- Convergent: Same people, survey + interview
- Pro: Direct individual-level integration
- Con: Participant burden, sequential delay

**2. Parallel sampling** (different but comparable)
- Convergent: Survey 500 employees, interview 20 different employees
- Pro: Larger quant sample, manageable qual
- Con: Can't link individual responses

**3. Nested sampling** (qual ⊂ quant)
- Explanatory: Survey 300, interview 15 from the 300
- Pro: Qual provides depth to quant participants
- Con: Qual may not represent all quant

**4. Multilevel sampling** (different units)
- Convergent: Survey individuals, interview teams
- Pro: Captures multiple levels
- Con: Complex integration across levels

---

### Integration Point Planning

**Four integration points to specify**:

**1. Design level** (How do methods relate?)
- Convergent: Independent data collection, merge at analysis
- Sequential: Phase 1 informs Phase 2 design
- Document: Timing, purpose, products

**2. Methods level** (How do instruments connect?)
- Convergent: Survey and interview guide developed independently
- Sequential: Phase 1 results → Phase 2 instrument
- Document: What transfers (themes, variables, cases)

**3. Interpretation level** (How do findings combine?)
- Joint display (table, matrix)
- Narrative weaving (side-by-side)
- Data transformation (quantitize or qualitize)
- Document: Integration strategy and tools

**4. Reporting level** (How are results presented?)
- Separate → integrated sections
- Integrated throughout
- Supplemental qual supporting quant
- Document: Structure and emphasis

## Design Selection Decision Tree

```
START: What is your research question?

├─ Do you have EQUAL priority for qual and quant?
│  ├─ YES → Can you collect simultaneously?
│  │  ├─ YES → CONVERGENT DESIGN
│  │  │  └─ Rationale: Triangulation or Expansion
│  │  └─ NO → Do you need development?
│  │     ├─ YES → EXPLORATORY SEQUENTIAL
│  │     └─ NO → EXPLANATORY SEQUENTIAL (choose based on what comes naturally first)
│  │
│  └─ NO → Which has higher priority?
│     │
│     ├─ QUANTITATIVE → EXPLANATORY SEQUENTIAL (QUAN → qual)
│     │  └─ Use qual to explain quant results
│     │
│     └─ QUALITATIVE → EXPLORATORY SEQUENTIAL (QUAL → quan)
│        └─ Use quan to test/generalize qual insights
```

## Common Design Selection Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Mixing just to mix

**Problem**: "We should do mixed methods because it's more rigorous"

**Reality**: Mixed methods only adds value if you have a question requiring both

**Fix**: Ask "What will mixing add?" If answer is "nothing specific", use single method

---

### Mistake 2: Wrong sequence

**Problem**: Choosing convergent when sequential would be better

**Symptoms**:
- Phase 1 data sits unused while Phase 2 collects
- Integration feels forced
- One method clearly more important

**Fix**: If one method informs the other or has priority, use sequential

---

### Mistake 3: Underestimating time

**Problem**: "We'll just add some interviews to the survey"

**Reality**: Mixed methods is not "survey + interviews", it's systematic integration

**Fix**: Budget for:
- Convergent: 1.5x time of longest method (due to integration)
- Sequential: Sum of both methods + connection time

---

### Mistake 4: No integration plan

**Problem**: "We'll figure out integration after we collect data"

**Reality**: Post-hoc integration rarely produces meaningful meta-inferences

**Fix**: Specify integration points during design phase:
- What will you integrate?
- How will you integrate?
- What tool (joint display, narrative, transformation)?

---

## Design Quality Checklist

**Before finalizing design, verify**:

- [ ] Research question requires both qual and quant (can't answer with one method)
- [ ] Design choice (convergent, sequential) matches timing and priority
- [ ] Rationale for mixing is clear (triangulation, complementarity, development, expansion)
- [ ] Integration points specified (design, methods, interpretation, reporting)
- [ ] Sample sizes appropriate for both methods
- [ ] Sampling strategy defined (identical, parallel, nested, multilevel)
- [ ] Timeline accounts for sequence (if applicable) and integration
- [ ] Resources available for both methods (skills, tools, time, budget)
- [ ] Plan for handling potential contradictions

## Quick Design Selector

**Use this quick reference to pick design**:

| Your Situation | Recommended Design |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Want comprehensive view, both methods equal | **Convergent** |
| Quant results need explanation | **Explanatory Sequential** |
| Need to develop measurement instrument | **Exploratory Sequential** |
| Want to validate findings across methods | **Convergent** |
| Understand why quant patterns exist | **Explanatory Sequential** |
| Test if qual themes generalize | **Exploratory Sequential** |
| Expand scope (quant outcomes + qual process) | **Convergent** |
| Select interview cases based on survey | **Explanatory Sequential** |
| Generate survey items from interviews | **Exploratory Sequential** |
