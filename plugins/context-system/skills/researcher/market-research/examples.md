## Example 1: SaaS Market Entry Assessment

### Context

**Business decision**: Should we enter the data quality tools market?

**Stakeholders**: CEO, Head of Product, VP Sales

**Timeline**: 3 weeks

---

### Process

**Phase 1: Define Objectives** (2 days)
- Decision: Enter market or not (go/no-go)
- Key questions:
  - What's the market size and growth?
  - Who are the top competitors?
  - What do customers need that isn't being met?
- Success criteria: Confidence on TAM ±20%, identify 10+ competitors

**Phase 2: Data Collection** (2 weeks)

**Secondary research** (1 week):
- Gartner report on data quality market ($2.1B, 12% CAGR)
- G2 categories: 47 vendors identified
- Competitor websites: Analyzed top 12
- Customer reviews: Mined 200+ G2/Capterra reviews

**Competitive analysis** (4 days):
- Deep dives on top 5 competitors
- Feature comparison across 15 dimensions
- Pricing analysis (range: $10k-$500k annually)
- Customer sentiment analysis

**Primary research** (1 week):
- Interviews with 12 potential customers
- Pain points: Integration complexity, false positives, expensive
- Willingness to switch: High if solves integration pain

**Market sizing**:
- TAM: $2.1B (Gartner)
- SAM: North America mid-market = $350M (17% of TAM)
- SOM: $15M in 3 years (5% of SAM, realistic capture rate)

**Phase 3: Analysis** (3 days)
- Synthesized findings: 5 key insights
- Positioning map: Identified "easy integration + affordable" gap
- Validated gap with customer interviews (9/12 wanted this)

**Phase 4: Communication** (2 days)
- Executive summary: Go-to-market recommended
- Detailed report: 15 pages
- Presentation to leadership: Approved

---

### Key Learnings

**What worked**:
- Triangulation (Gartner + reviews + interviews) gave high confidence
- Customer interviews validated gaps found in secondary research
- Positioning map made opportunity visual and clear

**What didn't**:
- Initial TAM estimate from one source - needed triangulation
- Underestimated time for competitor deep dives

**Outcome**: Decision to enter market, $5M seed funding approved

## Example 2: Competitive Repositioning

### Context

**Business decision**: How should we reposition against new competitor threatening market share?

**Stakeholders**: CMO, VP Product Marketing

**Timeline**: 2 weeks

---

### Process

**Phase 1: Define Objectives** (1 day)
- Decision: New positioning strategy
- Focus: Understand competitor's positioning and our differentiation
- Success: Clear positioning statement + messaging framework

**Phase 2: Data Collection** (1 week)

**Competitor deep dive** (3 days):
- Website analysis: Messaging, positioning, features
- Customer reviews: 50 G2 reviews analyzed
- Sales team interviews: 8 recent losses to competitor
- Mystery shopping: Went through competitor's sales process

**Customer research** (4 days):
- Interviews with 10 customers: Why they chose us
- Interviews with 5 lost deals: Why they chose competitor
- Survey of 150 customers: Feature importance rankings

**Phase 3: Analysis** (3 days)
- Identified competitor's positioning: "Enterprise-grade at mid-market price"
- Our strengths: Ease of use, time-to-value, support quality
- Weakness being exploited: Perception of "less powerful"
- Repositioning opportunity: "Fast value without complexity"

**Phase 4: Communication** (3 days)
- Positioning statement: "Get to insights in hours, not months"
- Messaging framework: 3 key messages + proof points
- Sales battlecard: When we win vs. when competitor wins
- Website rewrite: Launched new positioning

---

### Key Learnings

**What worked**:
- Mystery shopping revealed competitor's actual pitch (vs. website)
- Lost deal interviews most valuable (revealed real buying criteria)
- Sales team input critical for battlecard

**What didn't**:
- Initial focus too narrow (features only) - needed to understand buying psychology

**Outcome**: Repositioning launched, win rate vs. competitor increased from 35% to 55% over next quarter

## Example 3: Market Expansion Priority

### Context

**Business decision**: Which of 3 adjacent markets should we expand into first?

**Stakeholders**: CEO, Head of Sales

**Timeline**: 4 weeks

---

### Process

**Phase 1: Define Objectives** (2 days)
- Decision: Rank 3 markets (A, B, C) for expansion priority
- Criteria: Market size, competitive intensity, customer fit
- Success: Confidence on ranking with supporting evidence

**Phase 2: Data Collection** (2.5 weeks)

**Market A research** (1 week):
- Secondary: Industry reports, competitor analysis (8 players)
- Primary: 5 customer interviews
- Sizing: TAM $500M, SAM $80M

**Market B research** (1 week):
- Secondary: Less data available (emerging market)
- Primary: 8 customer interviews (more exploratory)
- Sizing: TAM $200M (estimated), SAM $40M

**Market C research** (1 week):
- Secondary: Mature market, lots of data
- Primary: 6 customer interviews
- Sizing: TAM $1.2B, SAM $150M

**Parallel analysis**:
- Competitive intensity scoring (1-10 for each)
- Customer fit assessment (overlap with current)
- Go-to-market feasibility (sales motion fit)

**Phase 3: Analysis** (3 days)

**Scoring framework** (weighted):
| Market | Size (30%) | Competition (25%) | Fit (25%) | Feasibility (20%) | **Total** |
|--------|------------|-------------------|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| A      | 7          | 6                 | 8         | 7                 | **7.0**   |
| B      | 4          | 8                 | 6         | 5                 | **5.8**   |
| C      | 9          | 3                 | 7         | 6                 | **6.5**   |

**Analysis**: Market A wins despite smaller size - best fit and feasibility

**Sensitivity analysis**: Tested different weightings, A still wins in 4/5 scenarios

**Phase 4: Communication** (2 days)
- Recommendation: Market A first, then C, defer B
- Rationale: Quick win in A builds confidence for larger C
- Market B too early (immature, uncertain)

---

### Key Learnings

**What worked**:
- Structured scoring prevented "analysis paralysis"
- Sensitivity analysis gave confidence in recommendation
- Customer interviews revealed fit insights not obvious from desk research

**What didn't**:
- Tried to be too comprehensive - could have been faster with tighter scope

**Outcome**: Expanded into Market A, achieved 30% of SAM ($24M) in 18 months

## Common Patterns

### Pattern 1: Triangulation Increases Confidence

All examples used multiple sources:
- Example 1: Gartner + reviews + interviews
- Example 2: Website + mystery shopping + customer interviews
- Example 3: Secondary + primary across all 3 markets

**Lesson**: Don't rely on single source. Agreement across sources = high confidence.

---

### Pattern 2: Customer Interviews Reveal Hidden Insights

All examples found critical insights in customer interviews:
- Example 1: Integration pain (not emphasized in competitor marketing)
- Example 2: "Fast value" positioning (from win/loss analysis)
- Example 3: Fit assessment (couldn't get from desk research alone)

**Lesson**: Secondary research shows "what", primary shows "why".

---

### Pattern 3: Visual Frameworks Aid Decision-Making

All examples used visual tools:
- Example 1: Positioning map (showed gap)
- Example 2: Battlecard (made differentiation clear)
- Example 3: Scoring matrix (made trade-offs explicit)

**Lesson**: Tables and visuals make insights actionable for stakeholders.

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Anti-Pattern 1: Analysis Paralysis

**Problem**: Endless data collection, no decision.

**Better**: Time-box research (2-4 weeks max), use scoring frameworks for decisions.

---

### Anti-Pattern 2: Confirmation Bias

**Problem**: Only seeking data that confirms hypothesis.

**Better**: Actively seek disconfirming evidence, interview customers who chose competitors.

---

### Anti-Pattern 3: Outdated Data

**Problem**: Using 3-year-old market sizing in fast-moving market.

**Better**: Prioritize recent sources (<1 year), validate with primary research.
