**Purpose**: Real-world culture research project walkthroughs across different approaches and purposes.

**Created**: 2026-02-08

## Example 1: Startup Culture Diagnostic (Structured Assessment - OCAI)

### Context

**Purpose**: Diagnostic - What is our current culture as we scale?

**Stakeholder**: CEO (company growing from 50 to 120 people in 1 year)

**Timeline**: 2 weeks

**Approach**: OCAI (Organizational Culture Assessment Instrument) + 10 interviews

---

### Phase 1: Define Objectives (Day 1-2)

**Research question**: "What is our culture now, and how should it evolve as we scale?"

**Rationale for OCAI**:
- Fast (2 weeks vs months for ethnography)
- Current vs preferred framework (shows change direction)
- Validated instrument (credibility with leadership)

**Scope**:
- All employees (n=120)
- Compare: Founders (n=5), early employees (<2 years, n=40), new hires (>2 years, n=75)

---

### Phase 2: Data Collection (Week 1)

**OCAI survey** (deployed Monday, closed Friday):
- Response rate: 92% (110/120)
- Time: 10 minutes per person
- Format: Online (Google Forms)

**Supplemental interviews** (10 participants):
- 3 founders/early leaders
- 4 mid-tenure employees
- 3 recent hires (<6 months)
- Questions: "How would you describe our culture?" "What's changed as we've grown?" "What should stay the same?"

---

### Phase 3: Analysis (Week 2)

**OCAI results** (averaged across all 110 respondents):

**Current culture scores** (out of 100 points distributed):
- **Adhocracy** (Create): 42 points (dominant)
- **Clan** (Collaborate): 31 points
- **Market** (Compete): 18 points
- **Hierarchy** (Control): 9 points

**Interpretation**: Strong Adhocracy culture (innovative, fast-moving, risk-taking) with Clan elements (family-like). Weak on Market (results focus) and Hierarchy (structure).

**Preferred culture scores**:
- **Adhocracy**: 38 points (slight decrease)
- **Clan**: 28 points (slight decrease)
- **Market**: 22 points (increase)
- **Hierarchy**: 12 points (increase)

**Gap analysis**: Employees want *slightly* more structure (Hierarchy) and results focus (Market) while maintaining innovation (Adhocracy).

---

**Sub-group comparison**:

| Group | Adhocracy (Now) | Hierarchy (Now) | Market (Now) | Clan (Now) |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|--------------|------------|
| **Founders** (n=5) | 48 | 5 | 25 | 22 |
| **Early employees** (n=40) | 45 | 8 | 16 | 31 |
| **New hires** (n=75) | 40 | 10 | 18 | 32 |

**Insight**: Founders emphasize innovation (Adhocracy 48) and results (Market 25). New hires want more structure (Hierarchy 10 vs founders 5). Early employees value collaboration (Clan 31) more than founders (Clan 22).

---

**Interview themes** (qualitative depth):

**Theme 1: "Scrappy startup" culture thriving but fragile** (8/10 participants):
- "We move fast and figure it out as we go"
- "Everyone wears multiple hats"
- "But as we grow, this starts to break down"

**Theme 2: Lack of process causing friction** (7/10 participants):
- "We don't have clear decision-making processes"
- "Sometimes three people are working on the same thing because no one coordinated"
- New hires: "I don't know who to ask for approval"

**Theme 3: Tension between speed and quality** (6/10 participants):
- Founders: "We need to keep moving fast to win market"
- New hires: "But we're accumulating technical debt"
- "Quality is suffering, customers are noticing"

---

### Phase 4: Reporting (Week 2)

**Report findings**:

**1. Current culture: Adhocracy-dominant** (innovation, speed, risk-taking)
- Evidence: OCAI Adhocracy = 42, interview themes ("move fast", "figure it out")
- Strength: Enables rapid product iteration, attracts innovative talent
- Challenge: Lacks structure as org scales (new hires feel lost)

**2. Desired evolution: Add structure WITHOUT killing innovation**
- Evidence: Preferred scores show +3 Hierarchy, +4 Market (not huge shifts)
- Employees don't want to become bureaucratic (Hierarchy still low at 12)
- But want basic processes (decision-making, coordination)

**3. Founder-employee culture gap emerging**
- Evidence: Founders emphasize Market (25) more than employees (16-18)
- Founders: Results-focused, competitive
- Employees: Collaboration-focused, "we're a family"
- Risk: As founders delegate, culture may drift from original vision

---

**Recommendations**:

**1. Introduce "minimal viable process"** (address Hierarchy gap):
- Decision-making framework (DACI: Driver, Approver, Contributor, Informed)
- Weekly cross-team sync (coordination without bureaucracy)
- Onboarding checklist (help new hires navigate culture)

**2. Balance innovation with quality** (address speed/quality tension):
- Definition of "done" includes quality bar (not just "shipped")
- Alternate sprints: Innovation sprint → quality/refactor sprint
- Customer feedback loop (ensure speed doesn't sacrifice user experience)

**3. Align on results focus** (close founder-employee gap):
- OKRs (Objectives & Key Results) shared company-wide
- Celebrate wins publicly (make Market culture more visible)
- Link innovation (Adhocracy) to customer outcomes (Market)

---

### Key Learnings

**What worked**:
- OCAI provided clear, visual culture profile (radar chart convinced CEO)
- Current vs preferred framework showed direction (not just current state)
- Sub-group analysis revealed founder-employee gap (actionable insight)
- Interviews explained "why" behind scores (made findings tangible)

**What didn't**:
- 10 interviews might be insufficient (wish we'd done 15-20 for more depth)
- OCAI doesn't capture everything (e.g., remote work culture not addressed)

**Outcome**:
- CEO implemented all 3 recommendations
- 6 months later: Re-assessed with OCAI
  - Hierarchy increased from 9 → 14 (mission accomplished)
  - Adhocracy stayed high at 40 (innovation preserved)
  - Market increased from 18 → 24 (results focus improved)
  - Employee satisfaction with "clarity" increased from 3.2 to 4.1/5.0

## Example 2: Post-Merger Culture Integration (Ethnographic Study)

### Context

**Purpose**: Change - Understand two cultures to guide integration

**Stakeholder**: Head of People (2 companies merged, struggling to integrate)

**Timeline**: 8 weeks

**Approach**: Ethnographic study (observation + interviews) across both legacy organizations

---

### Phase 1: Define Objectives (Week 1)

**Research question**: "What are the cultural differences between Company A and Company B, and how can we integrate successfully?"

**Rationale for ethnography**:
- Merger = sensitive (people may not answer survey honestly)
- Need deep understanding of assumptions and norms (not just espoused values)
- Culture clash already happening (need to understand "why")

**Scope**:
- **Company A** (acquirer, 300 people, 10 years old, SaaS)
- **Company B** (acquired, 80 people, 3 years old, startup)
- Focus: Engineering and Product teams (where integration is most critical)

---

### Phase 2: Data Collection (Week 2-6)

**Ethnographic observation** (4 weeks):

**Week 2-3: Observe Company A**:
- Attended 12 meetings (team standups, all-hands, planning)
- Shadowed 5 employees for full days
- Observed Slack culture (channels, communication norms)
- Photographed office space (open floor plan, conference rooms, whiteboards)

**Week 4-5: Observe Company B**:
- Attended 10 meetings (different format and cadence)
- Shadowed 4 employees
- Observed Slack culture (very different norms)
- Photographed office space (different setup, more casual)

**Field notes** (50+ pages):
- Detailed descriptions of meetings, interactions, language
- Noted discrepancies between stated values and observed behavior
- Tracked rituals (how decisions made, how failures handled)

---

**Interviews** (Week 3-6, n=25):
- **Company A**: 15 participants (managers, ICs, leadership)
- **Company B**: 10 participants
- Questions:
  - "How would you describe your company's culture?"
  - "What's valued here? What gets rewarded?"
  - "What's it like working with the other company post-merger?"
  - "What worries you about integration?"

---

**Document analysis**:
- Company A: Mission statements, employee handbook, internal memos
- Company B: Founding values document, Slack channel archives
- Both: Post-merger communications from leadership

---

### Phase 3: Analysis (Week 7)

**Cultural comparison** (Schein's levels):

**Artifacts** (observable):

| Artifact | Company A | Company B |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Office** | Structured (assigned desks, formal meeting rooms) | Flexible (hot desking, casual spaces) |
| **Dress** | Business casual (collared shirts) | Very casual (hoodies, t-shirts) |
| **Meetings** | Scheduled (Outlook invites, agendas) | Spontaneous (tap on shoulder, "got 5 min?") |
| **Communication** | Email-primary (formal) | Slack-primary (informal, GIFs) |
| **Rituals** | Weekly 1-on-1s (manager-driven) | Daily standups (team-driven) |

---

**Espoused values** (stated):

| Value | Company A (stated) | Company B (stated) |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Innovation** | "Customer-driven innovation" | "Move fast, break things" |
| **Collaboration** | "Cross-functional collaboration" | "Radical transparency" |
| **Quality** | "Operational excellence" | "Ship and iterate" |
| **Decision-making** | "Data-driven decisions" | "Trust your gut" |

---

**Enacted values** (observed behavior):

**Company A**:
- **Process-oriented**: Decisions go through review (design review, code review, launch review)
- **Hierarchy respected**: Managers have final say, ICs rarely challenge
- **Risk-averse**: "Let's de-risk this" is common phrase
- **Polish matters**: Products shipped when "perfect"

**Company B**:
- **Speed-oriented**: Decisions made quickly, iterate based on feedback
- **Flat hierarchy**: ICs challenge leadership openly, consensus-driven
- **Risk-tolerant**: "Let's try it and see" is common phrase
- **Iteration matters**: Products shipped when "good enough to learn from"

---

**Basic assumptions** (unconscious beliefs):

**Company A assumptions**:
- "Success comes from minimizing mistakes" (stability valued)
- "Experience equals wisdom" (seniority respected)
- "Customers want polished products" (quality over speed)
- "Process ensures quality" (structure necessary)

**Company B assumptions**:
- "Success comes from learning fast" (speed valued)
- "Good ideas come from anywhere" (meritocracy over seniority)
- "Customers want frequent updates" (speed over polish)
- "Process slows us down" (structure resisted)

---

**Culture clash symptoms** (from interviews + observation):

**Symptom 1: Decision-making friction** (18/25 participants mentioned):
- Company A: "Company B makes decisions without proper review, it's reckless"
- Company B: "Company A has too many meetings, we can't move fast"
- Observed: B engineers shipping code, A engineers blocking in code review ("needs more tests")

**Symptom 2: Communication breakdown** (15/25 participants):
- Company A: "Company B never uses email, I miss important updates in Slack"
- Company B: "Company A emails are walls of text, just tell me what you need"
- Observed: A sends formal email, B doesn't read it, A frustrated

**Symptom 3: Trust erosion** (12/25 participants):
- Company A: "Company B doesn't respect our processes, they think rules don't apply to them"
- Company B: "Company A micromanages us, they don't trust our judgment"
- Observed: A managers increasing oversight of B teams, B teams feeling stifled

---

### Phase 4: Reporting (Week 8)

**Report structure**:

**1. Two distinct cultures identified**:
- **Company A**: Process-driven, risk-averse, quality-focused, hierarchical (Hierarchy culture)
- **Company B**: Speed-driven, risk-tolerant, iteration-focused, flat (Adhocracy culture)

**2. Cultural assumptions conflict** (not just surface differences):
- A believes: Process ensures quality
- B believes: Process slows learning
- **These are deep assumptions, not easily changed**

**3. Integration approach must bridge assumptions**:
- Can't force B to adopt A's process (will kill what made B successful)
- Can't force A to abandon process (will create risk A can't tolerate)
- **Need hybrid model that preserves both**

---

**Recommendations**:

**1. Create "integration zones" (hybrid norms)**:
- **Critical systems** (production, security): Follow A's process (B agrees: "OK to be careful here")
- **Experimental features** (new products): Follow B's approach (A agrees: "OK to move fast here")
- **Shared work** (cross-company projects): Negotiate norms case-by-case

**2. Translate, don't force**:
- A wants reviews? → B can do lightweight reviews (not 3-stage process)
- B wants speed? → A can do rapid prototyping (in non-critical areas)
- **Find equivalents in each culture's language**

**3. Cultural ambassadors** (build bridges):
- Identify employees who appreciate both cultures
- A employees who value speed + B employees who value quality
- Pair them to co-lead projects (model integration)

**4. Leadership alignment on "one company" vision**:
- Define shared culture (not A or B, but "New Company")
- Articulate: What from A to keep, what from B to keep, what new to create
- Repeated communication (culture integration takes 12-18 months)

---

### Key Learnings

**What worked**:
- Ethnography revealed unconscious assumptions (surveys would have missed)
- Observing both cultures showed contrasts clearly
- Time investment (8 weeks) built trust (people shared honestly)
- Field notes provided rich evidence for report

**What didn't**:
- 8 weeks felt long to stakeholders (wanted faster results)
- Wish we'd involved leadership more during research (buy-in at end was harder)

**Outcome**:
- Leadership adopted "integration zones" approach
- 12 months later: Engagement scores improved from 3.1 to 3.8/5.0 (both companies)
- Attrition dropped (was spiking post-merger, now at normal levels)
- Cross-company projects increased from 2 to 12 (integration progressing)

## Example 3: Culture-Strategy Alignment (Combined Approach)

### Context

**Purpose**: Alignment - Does culture support our strategy?

**Stakeholder**: CEO + Strategy team (company pivoting from services to product)

**Timeline**: 6 weeks

**Approach**: Denison Survey (structured) + Ethnography (qualitative depth)

---

### Phase 1: Define Objectives (Week 1)

**Research question**: "Our strategy requires innovation and agility, but does our culture support this?"

**Context**: Company historically delivered custom services (stable, predictable). Now pivoting to product business (requires experimentation, iteration, speed).

**Hypothesis**: Culture is mis-aligned (still service-oriented, not product-oriented)

**Approach**:
- **Denison Survey**: Measures Mission, Adaptability, Involvement, Consistency
  - **Strategy requires**: High Adaptability (flexible, learning), high Involvement (empowered teams)
- **Ethnography**: Understand "why" culture might be mis-aligned

---

### Phase 2: Data Collection (Week 2-4)

**Denison Survey** (Week 2):
- All 250 employees invited
- Response rate: 78% (195 responses)
- Time: 15 minutes per person

**Ethnographic depth** (Week 2-4):
- **Observations**: 8 product team meetings, 3 leadership meetings
- **Interviews**: 15 participants (product leaders, engineers, former services team members)
- **Documents**: Old service contracts, new product roadmap, strategic memos

---

### Phase 3: Analysis (Week 5)

**Denison results**:

| Trait | Score (0-100) | Benchmark (Products) | Gap | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|-----|----------------|
| **Mission** | 68 | 70 | -2 | **Aligned** (clear direction) |
| **Adaptability** | 42 | 72 | **-30** | **Mis-aligned** (rigid, not learning) |
| **Involvement** | 55 | 68 | -13 | **Somewhat mis-aligned** (low empowerment) |
| **Consistency** | 74 | 65 | +9 | **Over-indexed** (too stable for product needs) |

**Interpretation**: Strong on Mission (knows where we're going) and Consistency (stable processes). **Weak on Adaptability** (can't pivot quickly) and **Involvement** (teams not empowered). Culture is still service-oriented (consistency valued over adaptation).

---

**Ethnographic findings explain "why"**:

**Theme 1: "Client-first" mindset persists** (12/15 interviews):
- Service culture: "Client wants X, we deliver X exactly"
- Product culture needs: "Users want Y, we experiment with Z to see if it's better than Y"
- Observed: Product teams still treating roadmap like client contract (deliver exactly as planned, no deviation)
- Quote: "We're afraid to say no to customers, even if feature request doesn't fit product vision"

**Theme 2: Approval-seeking behavior** (10/15 interviews):
- Service culture: Client approved every deliverable (milestones, sign-offs)
- Product culture needs: Teams experiment, learn fast, pivot without constant approval
- Observed: Product managers seeking executive approval for minor feature changes
- Quote: "We're used to getting sign-off on everything. Now we're supposed to just... decide? That feels risky."

**Theme 3: Failure aversion** (11/15 interviews):
- Service culture: Failure = lose client, financial penalty
- Product culture needs: Failure = learning (A/B tests, experiments)
- Observed: Teams avoiding experiments because "what if it doesn't work?"
- Quote: "In services, we couldn't afford to fail. That's hard to unlearn."

**Theme 4: Process over iteration** (9/15 interviews):
- Service culture: Defined processes (predictability for clients)
- Product culture needs: Rapid iteration (build-measure-learn)
- Observed: Teams following waterfall-like process even for product experiments
- Quote: "We have a 6-step approval process for launching features. By the time it's approved, market has changed."

---

**Integration** (joint display):

| Strategy Need | Denison Score (Reality) | Ethnographic Evidence (Why) | Gap |
|---------------|------------------------|----------------------------|-----|
| **Adaptability** (learn fast) | 42/100 (low) | Teams avoid experiments, seek approval, fear failure | **Large gap**: Service culture blocks adaptation |
| **Involvement** (empowered teams) | 55/100 (medium) | Approval-seeking behavior, teams wait for permission | **Medium gap**: Empowerment lacking |
| **Consistency** (stable processes) | 74/100 (high) | 6-step approval process, predictable delivery | **Over-indexed**: Too much process for product needs |

**Meta-inference**: Culture-strategy misalignment is rooted in service-to-product transition. Old culture (consistency, client-first, failure-averse) conflicts with new strategy (adaptation, learning, empowerment).

---

### Phase 4: Reporting (Week 6)

**Report findings**:

**1. Culture-strategy misalignment confirmed**:
- Strategy requires Adaptability (72 benchmark), culture scores 42 (-30 gap)
- Root cause: Service culture norms (client-first, approval-seeking, failure-averse) persist

**2. Three cultural barriers to product strategy**:
- **Barrier 1**: Client-first mindset prevents product vision focus
- **Barrier 2**: Approval-seeking slows iteration speed
- **Barrier 3**: Failure aversion prevents experimentation and learning

**3. Consistency is strength AND weakness**:
- Strength: Reliable delivery, customer trust
- Weakness: Over-indexed consistency blocks adaptation (74 vs 65 benchmark)

---

**Recommendations** (to close culture-strategy gap):

**1. Establish "product operating principles"** (shift from service mindset):
- "Vision over feature requests" (say no to customers if misaligned)
- "Learn through experiments" (celebrate learning, not just success)
- "Iterate, don't perfect" (ship 80% solution, improve based on feedback)
- **Action**: Leadership repeats these principles weekly (all-hands, team meetings)

**2. Decentralize decision-making** (increase Involvement):
- Product teams own roadmap (no executive approval for minor features)
- Establish decision rights framework (what teams decide vs what leadership decides)
- **Action**: Pilot with 2 product teams, measure time-to-decision before/after

**3. Ritualize learning from failure** (increase Adaptability):
- Monthly "Failure Fridays" (teams share experiments that didn't work, what they learned)
- A/B test norm (default to testing hypotheses, not opinions)
- **Action**: Product team leads champion this, track # of experiments per quarter

**4. Simplify approval process** (reduce over-indexed Consistency):
- 6-step process → 2-step for low-risk features (PM decides, engineer reviews)
- Reserve heavy process for high-risk (security, compliance, major architecture)
- **Action**: Revise product launch checklist, communicate clearly

---

### Key Learnings

**What worked**:
- Denison provided quantitative proof of misalignment (numbers convinced CEO)
- External benchmark showed gap (-30 on Adaptability) was significant
- Ethnography explained "why" (made abstract scores concrete with behaviors)
- Combined approach = comprehensive (quant + qual)

**What didn't**:
- 6 weeks felt rushed for ethnography (would have preferred 8-10 weeks for depth)
- Denison survey cost significant budget (but credibility was worth it)

**Outcome**:
- CEO prioritized culture change as strategic imperative
- All 4 recommendations implemented over 12 months
- Re-assessed with Denison at 12 months:
  - Adaptability: 42 → 58 (+16, still below benchmark but improving)
  - Involvement: 55 → 64 (+9, approaching benchmark)
  - Consistency: 74 → 68 (-6, reduced unnecessary process)
- Product velocity increased 40% (time from idea to launch)

## Common Patterns Across Examples

### Pattern 1: Structured Tools Provide Baseline, Ethnography Explains "Why"

All examples show value of combining approaches:
- **Example 1**: OCAI showed culture profile, interviews explained themes
- **Example 2**: Pure ethnography (but could have used OCAI to quantify differences)
- **Example 3**: Denison quantified gap, ethnography explained root causes

**Lesson**: Structured tools give numbers (credibility, tracking), ethnography gives meaning (actionability).

---

### Pattern 2: Sub-Cultures Exist (Compare Groups)

All examples found variation within organizations:
- **Example 1**: Founders vs early employees vs new hires (different culture preferences)
- **Example 2**: Company A vs Company B (post-merger sub-cultures)
- **Example 3**: Product teams vs legacy services teams (different mindsets)

**Lesson**: Don't assume culture is uniform. Compare sub-groups to identify tensions.

---

### Pattern 3: Culture Change Takes Time (Track Over Time)

All examples tracked culture over time:
- **Example 1**: Re-assessed at 6 months (Hierarchy increased as intended)
- **Example 2**: 12 months to see integration progress (engagement improved)
- **Example 3**: 12 months to measure culture shift (Adaptability improved +16)

**Lesson**: Culture change is slow (6-18 months). Re-assess to track progress.

---

### Pattern 4: Leadership Action Required (Assessment Alone Doesn't Change Culture)

All examples resulted in specific actions:
- **Example 1**: Minimal viable process, OKRs, quality sprints
- **Example 2**: Integration zones, cultural ambassadors, leadership alignment
- **Example 3**: Product principles, decentralized decisions, failure rituals

**Lesson**: Don't just diagnose culture. Specify actions leadership will take.

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Anti-Pattern 1: Survey Without Follow-Up

**Problem**: Deploy survey, share results, do nothing

**Better**: Plan actions before you survey. "If we find X, we'll do Y."

---

### Anti-Pattern 2: Blaming Culture for Everything

**Problem**: "Our culture is broken" (vague, discouraging)

**Better**: Be specific. "Our culture values consistency, but strategy requires adaptability." (actionable)

---

### Anti-Pattern 3: Assuming One "Right" Culture

**Problem**: "We need to be more innovative" (based on generic advice)

**Better**: Align culture to strategy. "Our strategy requires X, so culture needs Y."

---

### Anti-Pattern 4: Ignoring Sub-Cultures

**Problem**: Report single culture profile for 500-person org

**Better**: Analyze by sub-group (team, function, location). Surface differences.
