# Organizational & Culture Research Workflow

**Purpose**: Understand organizational culture, dynamics, and behavior through structured assessment and ethnographic observation.

**Version**: 1.0
**Created**: 2026-02-08
**Status**: Production

---

## Overview

Organizational and culture research combines structured assessment tools (quantitative) with ethnographic observation (qualitative) to understand how organizations function, what values guide behavior, and how culture enables or constrains performance.

**Core principle**: Culture is enacted, not just stated. Observe what people do, not just what they say.

**When to use**: Culture assessment, organizational diagnosis, change initiatives, understanding team dynamics, identifying cultural barriers.

## What is Organizational Culture Research?

### Core Concept

**Organizational culture** = Shared values, beliefs, norms, and practices that shape behavior

**Three levels of culture** (Edgar Schein):

1. **Artifacts** (Observable)
   - Physical: Office layout, dress code, tools
   - Behavioral: Rituals, language, interactions
   - **Easy to observe, hard to interpret**

2. **Espoused Values** (Stated)
   - Mission statements, company values, leadership messages
   - What organization says it values
   - **May or may not align with enacted values**

3. **Basic Assumptions** (Unconscious)
   - Deeply held beliefs about reality, truth, time, relationships
   - Taken for granted, invisible
   - **Hardest to surface, most powerful**

**Goal**: Understand all three levels and how they align or conflict.

---

### Two Approaches

**1. Structured Assessment** (Quantitative + qualitative)
- Use validated instruments (OCAI, Denison, etc.)
- Survey all employees, analyze results
- Fast (1-2 weeks), standardized, comparable
- **Use when**: Want quantifiable culture profile, benchmark against norms, track change over time

**2. Ethnographic Study** (Qualitative depth)
- Immersive observation, interviews, document analysis
- Deep understanding of meaning and context
- Slow (4-8 weeks), rich, contextual
- **Use when**: Need to understand "why" behind culture, surfacing unconscious assumptions, new or poorly understood culture

**Best practice**: Combine both (mixed methods) for comprehensive understanding.

---

### Culture vs Climate vs Engagement

**Culture**: Shared values and norms (what's valued, how we work)
**Climate**: Perceptions of work environment (how it feels to work here)
**Engagement**: Emotional commitment to organization (how motivated people are)

**Relationship**: Culture shapes climate, climate influences engagement.

**This workflow focuses on culture** (values, norms, practices), with climate and engagement as related outcomes.

## Four-Phase Workflow

### Timeline

**Structured assessment**: 1-2 weeks
**Ethnographic study**: 4-8 weeks
**Combined approach**: 5-10 weeks

**Phase breakdown**:
- Phase 1: Define Objectives (1-2 days)
- Phase 2: Data Collection (1-8 weeks depending on approach)
- Phase 3: Analysis (3-7 days)
- Phase 4: Reporting (2-3 days)

---

## Phase 1: Define Objectives

**Goal**: Clarify what you want to understand about culture and why.

**Duration**: 1-2 days

---

### Activities

**1. Identify research purpose** (2-3 hours)

**Five common purposes**:

**Diagnostic**: What is our current culture?
- Use when: Leadership wants to understand culture as-is
- Example: "Describe our culture across 4 dimensions"

**Change**: How does culture need to evolve?
- Use when: Org is changing (growth, merger, transformation)
- Example: "What cultural barriers prevent innovation?"

**Alignment**: Does culture support strategy?
- Use when: Strategy/culture fit is questioned
- Example: "Our strategy requires risk-taking, but is our culture risk-averse?"

**Comparison**: How do sub-cultures differ?
- Use when: Multiple teams/locations with different cultures
- Example: "How does engineering culture differ from sales culture?"

**Intervention**: Did culture change initiative work?
- Use when: Measuring impact of culture intervention (training, new practices)
- Example: "Did our psychological safety training change culture?"

---

**2. Choose research approach** (1-2 hours)

**Decision tree**:

```
What is your timeline and depth need?

├─ Fast assessment (1-2 weeks) + quantifiable results?
│  └─ Structured Assessment (OCAI, Denison, or custom survey)
│
├─ Deep understanding (4-8 weeks) + rich context?
│  └─ Ethnographic Study (observation, interviews, immersion)
│
└─ Comprehensive (5-10 weeks) + triangulation?
   └─ Combined Approach (structured tool + ethnographic depth)
```

**See**: [[culture-assessment-tools]] for tool selection guidance

---

**3. Define scope and boundaries** (1-2 hours)

**Scope questions**:
- **Who**: Entire org? Specific teams? Leadership only?
- **What aspects**: All of culture or specific dimensions (e.g., innovation, collaboration)?
- **Comparison**: Single snapshot or comparing across units/time?

**Example scope**:
- "Assess innovation culture across engineering org (120 people) using OCAI + 15 interviews"

---

**4. Plan stakeholder engagement** (1 hour)

**Key stakeholders**:
- **Sponsor**: Who requested this research? (keep informed)
- **Leadership**: Who will act on findings? (involve in design)
- **Participants**: Who will be surveyed/interviewed? (communicate purpose)
- **Influencers**: Who can champion change based on findings?

**Engagement plan**:
- Kick-off: Share purpose and approach
- Data collection: Communicate expectations and confidentiality
- Results: Who sees what, when, how

---

### Phase 1 Outputs

- ✅ Research purpose defined (diagnostic, change, alignment, comparison, intervention)
- ✅ Approach selected (structured, ethnographic, or combined)
- ✅ Scope and boundaries clear
- ✅ Stakeholder engagement plan

---

## Phase 2: Data Collection

**Goal**: Gather data about culture through chosen approach.

**Duration**: 1-8 weeks (varies by approach)

---

### Approach A: Structured Assessment (1-2 weeks)

**Use validated culture assessment instruments.**

**Step 1: Select instrument** (see [[culture-assessment-tools]]):

**OCAI (Organizational Culture Assessment Instrument)**:
- Measures culture across 4 types (Clan, Adhocracy, Market, Hierarchy)
- 6 dimensions, 24 items
- Time: 10 minutes per person
- Output: Culture profile (current + preferred)

**Denison Organizational Culture Survey**:
- Measures 4 traits (Mission, Adaptability, Involvement, Consistency)
- 60 items
- Time: 15 minutes per person
- Output: Culture score + external benchmark

**Custom survey**:
- Tailored to specific culture dimensions
- Developed from interviews or theory
- Time: 10-20 minutes per person
- Output: Custom culture profile

---

**Step 2: Deploy survey** (3-5 days):
- Communicate purpose and confidentiality
- Send survey to all participants (aim for 70%+ response rate)
- Send reminders (day 3, day 5)
- Close survey after 1 week

---

**Step 3: Collect supplemental qualitative data** (optional, 1 week):
- 5-10 interviews asking "Why do you think culture is this way?"
- Provides context for survey results
- Helps interpret unexpected findings

---

### Approach B: Ethnographic Study (4-8 weeks)

**Immersive observation and deep interviews.**

**Step 1: Gain access and build rapport** (1-2 weeks):
- Attend team meetings, observe daily work
- Informal conversations (build trust)
- Shadow participants during typical activities
- Goal: Become "part of the furniture" (people act naturally)

---

**Step 2: Systematic observation** (2-4 weeks):

**What to observe** (Schein's levels):

**Artifacts**:
- Physical space (open office? private offices? remote?)
- Dress (formal? casual?)
- Language (jargon, acronyms, metaphors)
- Rituals (all-hands, standups, celebrations)
- Tools (Slack culture, email culture, async vs sync)

**Espoused values**:
- Mission/values on website or walls
- Leadership messages (all-hands, memos)
- What people say is valued ("we value transparency")

**Enacted values** (behaviors):
- How decisions are actually made (who has voice?)
- How conflicts are handled (open debate? avoid?)
- How failures are treated (blame? learning?)
- How time is spent (meetings? deep work? social?)

**Field notes template**:
```markdown
**Date**: [Date]
**Setting**: [Where, who present]
**Observation**: [What happened - descriptive, not interpretive]
**Artifact**: [Physical evidence - photo, document, quote]
**Interpretation**: [What might this mean culturally?]
**Question**: [Follow-up questions to explore]
```

---

**Step 3: In-depth interviews** (2-3 weeks):
- 15-25 participants (diverse roles, tenure, perspectives)
- 60-90 minute interviews
- Questions:
  - "Describe a typical day here"
  - "How do people get recognized and rewarded?"
  - "Tell me about a time someone broke an unwritten rule - what happened?"
  - "If a new person joined, what would they need to know to fit in?"
  - "What do we do here that would seem strange to outsiders?"

---

**Step 4: Document analysis** (ongoing):
- Internal: Emails, Slack archives, meeting notes, memos
- External: Website, blog, social media, press
- Look for: Stated values, decision rationales, language patterns

---

### Approach C: Combined (Recommended for Comprehensive Study)

**Phase 1** (Week 1-2): Structured assessment
- Deploy OCAI or Denison survey
- Get quantitative culture profile

**Phase 2** (Week 3-6): Ethnographic depth
- Use survey results to focus ethnography
- Example: If survey shows "low innovation", observe how new ideas are treated
- Interviews explore "why" behind survey scores

**Integration**: Quantitative shows "what" culture is, qualitative explains "why" and "how"

---

### Phase 2 Outputs

**Structured assessment**:
- ✅ Survey results (response rate, scores by dimension)
- ✅ Qualitative interview notes (if collected)

**Ethnographic study**:
- ✅ Field notes from observations (organized by date or theme)
- ✅ Interview transcripts or detailed notes (15-25 interviews)
- ✅ Document archive (internal + external)

**Combined**:
- ✅ Both sets of outputs

---

## Phase 3: Analysis

**Goal**: Synthesize data to create culture profile and insights.

**Duration**: 3-7 days (varies by approach)

---

### Structured Assessment Analysis (2-3 days)

**Step 1: Calculate scores** (1 day):
- **OCAI**: Average scores across 4 culture types (Clan, Adhocracy, Market, Hierarchy)
- **Denison**: Average scores across 4 traits (Mission, Adaptability, Involvement, Consistency)
- **Custom**: Descriptive statistics by dimension

**Step 2: Visualize profile** (1 day):
- Culture profile chart (radar chart or bar chart)
- Current vs preferred (for OCAI)
- Comparison across sub-groups (if applicable)

**Step 3: Interpret findings** (1 day):
- What is dominant culture type/trait?
- Where are strengths and weaknesses?
- Gaps between current and preferred?
- Differences across teams/roles?

---

### Ethnographic Analysis (5-7 days)

**Step 1: Organize data** (1 day):
- Compile field notes, interview transcripts, documents
- Create chronological or thematic folders

**Step 2: Thematic coding** (2-3 days):
- Code field notes and interviews (using Schein's levels or emergent themes)
- Example codes: "Decision-making norms", "Failure handling", "Communication patterns"
- Group codes into themes

**Step 3: Identify cultural patterns** (1-2 days):

**Patterns to look for**:
- **Rituals**: Repeated behaviors with symbolic meaning (e.g., weekly demos as innovation ritual)
- **Norms**: Unwritten rules about what's acceptable (e.g., "don't email after 6pm")
- **Language**: Jargon, metaphors, stories that reveal values (e.g., "move fast" = value speed)
- **Power dynamics**: Who has influence, how authority works (e.g., flat hierarchy in name, but CEO has final say)
- **Conflicts**: Espoused vs enacted values (e.g., say "transparency", but decisions made in private)

**Step 4: Build cultural narrative** (1-2 days):
- Describe culture across Schein's levels (artifacts, values, assumptions)
- Link observations to interpretations (with evidence)
- Identify core cultural themes (3-5 defining characteristics)

---

### Combined Analysis (Integration)

**Create joint display**:
| Culture Dimension | Survey Score | Ethnographic Evidence | Integration |
|-------------------|--------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Innovation | 3.2/5.0 (low) | Observed: New ideas dismissed in meetings, "that's not how we do things" | **Confirms**: Culture resists new ideas |
| Collaboration | 4.1/5.0 (high) | Observed: Frequent spontaneous collaboration, open Slack channels | **Confirms**: Strong collaborative norms |

**Meta-inferences**: Insights that emerge from combining methods

---

### Phase 3 Outputs

- ✅ Culture profile (quantitative or narrative or both)
- ✅ Thematic analysis (3-5 core themes)
- ✅ Patterns identified (rituals, norms, conflicts, power dynamics)
- ✅ Integration (if combined approach)

---

## Phase 4: Reporting

**Goal**: Communicate culture findings to stakeholders.

**Duration**: 2-3 days

---

### Report Structure

**Culture assessment report** (10-20 pages):

1. **Executive Summary** (1 page)
   - 3-5 key findings
   - Implications for organization
   - Top recommendations

2. **Purpose & Methodology** (1-2 pages)
   - Research question
   - Approach (structured, ethnographic, combined)
   - Participants and data sources
   - Limitations

3. **Culture Profile** (3-5 pages)
   - **Quantitative**: Culture scores, visualizations (if structured assessment)
   - **Qualitative**: Narrative description, themes (if ethnographic)
   - **Integrated**: Joint display (if combined)

4. **Key Findings** (3-5 pages)
   - Finding 1: [Theme with evidence]
   - Finding 2: [Theme with evidence]
   - Finding 3: [Theme with evidence]
   - [3-5 findings total]

5. **Strengths & Challenges** (2-3 pages)
   - What cultural strengths exist? (to leverage)
   - What cultural challenges exist? (to address)

6. **Alignment with Strategy** (1-2 pages) *(if relevant)*
   - Does culture support strategic goals?
   - Where are gaps?

7. **Recommendations** (2-3 pages)
   - Leverage strengths (what to amplify)
   - Address challenges (what to change)
   - Specific actions with owners and timelines

8. **Appendices**
   - Survey instrument (if used)
   - Interview protocol
   - Additional data visualizations

---

### Presentation Format

**For leadership** (20-30 slides):

1. **Research purpose and approach** (2 slides)
2. **Culture profile overview** (1-2 slides with visualization)
3. **Key findings** (1 slide per finding, 3-5 slides total)
4. **Strengths to leverage** (1-2 slides)
5. **Challenges to address** (1-2 slides)
6. **Recommendations** (2-3 slides with specific actions)
7. **Q&A**

**Visual tips**:
- Use culture profile chart (radar, bar, or heat map)
- Include representative quotes from interviews
- Show evidence for each finding (quote + observation + data)
- Make recommendations concrete (not "improve collaboration", but "establish weekly cross-team syncs")

---

### Phase 4 Outputs

- ✅ Culture assessment report (10-20 pages)
- ✅ Leadership presentation (20-30 slides)
- ✅ Recommendations with action plan

## Specialized Techniques

### Culture Change Assessment

**Purpose**: Measure if culture changed after intervention

**Approach**:
- **Baseline**: Assess culture before intervention (OCAI or ethnography)
- **Intervention**: Implement change (new practices, training, leadership)
- **Follow-up**: Re-assess culture 6-12 months later (same instrument)
- **Compare**: Current vs baseline, quantify change

**Example**:
- Baseline (Jan 2024): Psychological safety = 3.1/5.0
- Intervention (Feb-June 2024): Manager training on feedback, team retros established
- Follow-up (Dec 2024): Psychological safety = 4.0/5.0
- Conclusion: Culture shift detected, intervention likely contributed

---

### Sub-Culture Comparison

**Purpose**: Compare culture across teams, locations, or functions

**Approach**:
- Deploy same assessment to multiple sub-groups
- Analyze scores by group
- Identify where sub-cultures differ

**Example**:
- Engineering: High on innovation (4.2), low on hierarchy (2.8)
- Sales: Low on innovation (3.0), high on market orientation (4.5)
- Insight: Different functions have different cultures (sub-cultures exist)

---

### Critical Incident Analysis

**Purpose**: Understand culture through response to critical events

**Approach**:
- Identify critical incident (crisis, failure, success, change)
- Interview participants about how org responded
- Analyze response for cultural patterns (what was valued? how were decisions made?)

**Example**:
- Critical incident: Product launch failed
- Response: Public post-mortem (transparency), no blame (learning culture), team given autonomy to fix (trust)
- Insight: Culture values learning over blame, transparency over hiding failures

## Troubleshooting

### "Survey response rate is low (<50%)"

**Symptom**: Not enough responses for reliable assessment

**Causes**: Survey fatigue, lack of trust, unclear purpose, timing

**Solutions**:
1. Communicate purpose clearly (what will be done with results)
2. Guarantee confidentiality (anonymize responses)
3. Get leadership endorsement (CEO sends survey, emphasizes importance)
4. Keep survey short (<15 min)
5. Send reminders (day 3, day 5, day 7)
6. Offer incentives (e.g., team with highest response rate gets lunch)

---

### "Can't access organization for ethnography"

**Symptom**: Gatekeepers block access, people won't talk, can't observe

**Causes**: Lack of trust, political sensitivity, confidentiality concerns

**Solutions**:
1. Build rapport first (informal conversations before formal observation)
2. Guarantee anonymity (no names in reports, aggregate data)
3. Start with willing participants (early adopters, then expand)
4. Use insider researcher (someone from org conducts research with guidance)
5. Fall back on structured assessment + interviews (less intrusive than ethnography)

---

### "Espoused vs enacted values conflict (say X, do Y)"

**Symptom**: What people say culture is differs from observed behavior

**Cause**: This is common! Espoused values are aspirational, enacted values are real.

**Solutions**:
1. Don't ignore conflict - it's a key finding
2. Report as: "Organization aspires to X (espoused) but current behavior shows Y (enacted)"
3. Explore why gap exists (structural barriers? leadership behavior? incentives?)
4. Recommendations: Align espoused and enacted (change espoused to match reality, or change practices to match espoused)

**Example**:
- Espoused: "We value work-life balance" (on website)
- Enacted: Late-night Slack messages, weekend work expected (observed)
- Gap: Leadership says balance but models overwork
- Recommendation: Leadership commit to boundaries (no Slack after 6pm, model taking vacation)

## Known Limitations

**Culture research cannot**:
- Definitively prove causality (culture → performance) without experimental design
- Change culture by itself (requires intervention, not just assessment)
- Fully capture unconscious assumptions (some will remain hidden)

**Culture research requires**:
- **Trust**: Participants must feel safe to share honestly
- **Time**: Quick assessments miss depth, deep ethnography takes months
- **Context**: Culture is context-dependent (findings may not generalize)
- **Interpretation**: Culture is interpreted (researcher bias affects conclusions)

**Culture research excels when**:
- Need to diagnose current culture (baseline)
- Want to understand cultural enablers or barriers to goals
- Measuring culture change over time
- Comparing sub-cultures across organization
