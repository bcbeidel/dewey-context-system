# Case Study Research Workflow

**Purpose**: In-depth investigation of contemporary phenomena in real-world context using multiple sources of evidence.

**Version**: 1.0
**Created**: 2026-02-08
**Status**: Production

---

## Overview

Case study research investigates a bounded system (case) in depth using multiple data sources to understand complexity, context, and uniqueness.

**Core principle**: Depth over breadth. Rich, contextualized understanding of specific cases rather than generalizable patterns.

**When to use**: Real-world phenomena in context, "how" and "why" questions, contemporary events you can observe, need for contextual depth.

## What is Case Study Research?

### Core Concept

**Case study** = In-depth investigation of bounded system using multiple evidence sources

**Bounded system** = Defined boundaries of time, space, activity
- Organization (e.g., "Company X's remote work transition")
- Program (e.g., "Onboarding program at Startup Y")
- Individual (e.g., "CEO's decision-making during crisis")
- Event (e.g., "Product launch failure")
- Process (e.g., "How Team Z adopted new technology")

**Multiple evidence** = Triangulate across:
- Interviews
- Documents
- Observations
- Artifacts
- Quantitative data

---

### Case Study vs Other Methods

**Case study is NOT**:
- ❌ Single interview (need multiple sources)
- ❌ Anecdote or story (need systematic investigation)
- ❌ Survey of many cases (that's cross-sectional research)

**Case study IS**:
- ✅ Deep dive into specific bounded case
- ✅ Multiple data sources triangulated
- ✅ Context-dependent understanding
- ✅ Rich description of complexity

---

### Three Types of Case Studies (Robert Yin)

**1. Exploratory** - "What's happening here?"
- Purpose: Explore new phenomena, generate hypotheses
- Use when: Little existing knowledge, phenomenon emerging
- Example: "How do early-stage startups manage without HR departments?"

**2. Descriptive** - "What does this case look like?"
- Purpose: Describe phenomenon in real-world context
- Use when: Want rich description, document best/worst practices
- Example: "How does Company X's culture enable innovation?"

**3. Explanatory** - "Why did this happen?"
- Purpose: Explain causal mechanisms and relationships
- Use when: Seeking to understand why outcomes occurred
- Example: "Why did Product Launch Y fail despite positive user research?"

**Source**: [[../../context/research/case-study-research-standards]]

---

### Single vs Multiple Case Designs

**Single-case design**:
- One case investigated deeply
- Use when: Critical, unique, typical, revelatory, or longitudinal case
- Example: "Atlassian's 20-year remote work evolution"

**Multiple-case design**:
- 2-10 cases compared
- Use when: Want replication logic, contrast patterns, build theory
- Example: "Comparing 5 companies' AI adoption strategies"

**Embedded units**:
- Sub-units within cases
- Example: Single case (Company X) with embedded units (3 teams within company)

## Four-Phase Workflow

### Timeline

**Typical case study project**: 3-8 weeks (single case), 8-16 weeks (multiple cases)

**Phase breakdown**:
- Phase 1: Case Selection & Design (2-3 days)
- Phase 2: Data Collection (2-6 weeks)
- Phase 3: Within-Case Analysis (1-2 weeks per case)
- Phase 4: Cross-Case Analysis & Reporting (1-2 weeks)

---

## Phase 1: Case Selection & Design

**Goal**: Define case boundaries, select cases, and plan data collection.

**Duration**: 2-3 days

---

### Activities

**1. Define research question** (1-2 hours)

Case studies answer **"how"** and **"why"** questions:

**Good case study questions**:
- "How did Company X successfully transition to remote-first?"
- "Why did startup Y's growth strategy fail?"
- "How do high-performing teams coordinate across time zones?"

**Bad questions** (other methods better):
- "What percentage of companies are remote-first?" (survey)
- "Does remote work affect productivity?" (experiment)

---

**2. Determine case type** (1 hour)

**Exploratory**: Little known, generate hypotheses
- "What does AI-native product development look like?" (new phenomenon)

**Descriptive**: Rich description of phenomenon
- "Describe Basecamp's approach to work-life boundaries" (document practices)

**Explanatory**: Understand causality
- "Why did Company Z's reorganization succeed when others failed?" (causal mechanisms)

---

**3. Select case(s)** (2-4 hours)

**Single-case selection criteria** (choose one or more):

**Critical case**: Tests well-formulated theory
- "If remote work fails here (optimal conditions), it won't work anywhere"

**Unique case**: Rare or extreme situation
- "Only company to successfully exit from PE ownership to employee-owned"

**Typical case**: Represents common circumstances
- "Mid-sized SaaS company facing typical growth challenges"

**Revelatory case**: Previously inaccessible phenomenon
- "First-ever look inside Company X's innovation process"

**Longitudinal case**: Study over time
- "Company Y's 5-year journey from startup to scale-up"

---

**Multiple-case selection** (2-10 cases):

**Literal replication**: Cases predict similar results
- "5 companies that successfully adopted 4-day work week" (all succeeded, learn commonalities)

**Theoretical replication**: Cases predict contrasting results for predictable reasons
- "3 successful + 3 failed remote transitions" (contrast patterns)

**Key principle**: Cases are not a sample (not statistical generalization). Each case is a test of theoretical propositions.

---

**4. Define case boundaries** (1-2 hours)

**Boundaries** = What's included vs excluded

**Time**: When does case start and end?
- "Company X's remote transition: March 2020 - December 2021"

**Space**: What locations or units?
- "Engineering and Product teams (exclude Sales)"

**Activity**: What phenomena?
- "Focus on collaboration practices, not performance outcomes"

**Clear boundaries prevent scope creep.**

---

**5. Plan data sources** (2-3 hours)

**Six sources of evidence** (use multiple for triangulation):

**1. Interviews** (primary qualitative)
- Who: Key stakeholders (5-20 people)
- How: Semi-structured, 45-60 min
- What: Experiences, perspectives, explanations

**2. Documents** (secondary qualitative)
- Internal: Memos, emails, Slack archives, meeting notes
- External: Press releases, blog posts, social media
- What: Official positions, decisions, chronology

**3. Archival records** (secondary quantitative)
- HR data, financial records, usage analytics
- What: Objective measures, trends, outcomes

**4. Direct observations** (primary qualitative)
- Attend meetings, shadow participants, observe interactions
- What: Actual behavior vs reported behavior

**5. Participant observation** (primary qualitative)
- Researcher actively participates in case
- What: Insider perspective, tacit knowledge

**6. Physical artifacts** (secondary)
- Products, tools, office layouts, screenshots
- What: Material evidence of culture and practices

**Plan**: Which sources for which research questions? Schedule and access?

---

### Phase 1 Outputs

- ✅ Research question defined ("how" or "why")
- ✅ Case type selected (exploratory, descriptive, explanatory)
- ✅ Case(s) selected with clear boundaries
- ✅ Data collection plan (sources, schedule, access)

---

## Phase 2: Data Collection

**Goal**: Gather evidence from multiple sources to build comprehensive case understanding.

**Duration**: 2-6 weeks (varies by case complexity and access)

---

### Principles of Case Study Data Collection

**1. Use multiple sources** - Triangulation increases validity
**2. Create case study database** - Organize evidence systematically
**3. Maintain chain of evidence** - Link conclusions to evidence to questions
**4. Be opportunistic** - Follow interesting leads that emerge

---

### Data Collection Activities

**Interviews** (1-3 weeks):
- **Protocol**: Semi-structured with flexible follow-up
- **Sample**: 5-20 participants (key stakeholders + diverse perspectives)
- **Process**:
  - Start with broad questions ("Tell me about...")
  - Follow interesting themes
  - Ask for examples and evidence
  - Triangulate across interviews

**Document collection** (ongoing):
- Request access to internal documents
- Collect public documents (websites, press, social media)
- Archive as collected (with dates and sources)
- Index by theme or chronology

**Observations** (1-4 weeks):
- Attend key meetings or events
- Shadow participants during typical activities
- Note discrepancies (what people say vs do)
- Photograph or sketch physical spaces/artifacts

**Archival data** (1-2 weeks):
- Request quantitative data (performance, usage, financials)
- Organize by time period or unit
- Create visualizations if helpful

---

### Case Study Database

**Organize evidence systematically**:

```
/case-study-[topic]/
├── raw-data/
│   ├── interviews/ (transcripts or detailed notes)
│   ├── documents/ (PDFs, archives)
│   ├── observations/ (field notes)
│   ├── archival/ (data files, spreadsheets)
│   └── artifacts/ (photos, screenshots)
├── case-study-protocol.md (research questions, data collection plan)
├── chronology.md (timeline of key events)
├── case-narrative.md (evolving story)
└── analysis-notes.md (emerging themes, connections)
```

**Why database?**: Audit trail, enable secondary analysis, support replication

---

### Phase 2 Outputs

- ✅ Interviews completed and transcribed/noted
- ✅ Documents collected and archived
- ✅ Observations documented
- ✅ Quantitative data gathered
- ✅ Case study database organized

---

## Phase 3: Within-Case Analysis

**Goal**: Analyze each case individually to build rich description and identify patterns.

**Duration**: 1-2 weeks per case

---

### Within-Case Analysis Process

**1. Create chronology** (1-2 days)

**Timeline of key events**:
- What happened, when, who involved, what evidence
- Helps establish causal sequence
- Reveals turning points

**Example chronology entry**:
```markdown
**March 15, 2020**: CEO announces office closure (COVID-19)
- Source: Company-wide email, CEO interview
- Decision made in 48 hours after emergency leadership meeting
- All employees transitioned to remote by March 20
```

---

**2. Write case narrative** (2-3 days)

**Rich description** of case:
- Context: What was the situation?
- Actors: Who were the key people?
- Actions: What happened? (chronological or thematic)
- Outcomes: What resulted?

**Use multiple evidence sources** in narrative:
- "The CEO described the decision as 'the hardest of my career' (interview, 2021). Internal emails from that week show leadership debating three options (documents). The announcement was made at an all-hands meeting, where employees expressed anxiety but also relief (observation notes)."

**Goal**: Reader should feel like they "were there"

---

**3. Identify themes and patterns** (2-3 days)

**Thematic coding** similar to qualitative research:
- Code interview transcripts, documents, observation notes
- Group codes into categories
- Identify cross-cutting themes
- Link themes to research questions

**Example themes** (from remote transition case):
- Communication challenges (12 codes)
- Loss of spontaneous collaboration (8 codes)
- Work-life boundary erosion (10 codes)
- Increased autonomy (7 codes)

---

**4. Build explanation** (1-2 days)

**For explanatory cases**: Identify causal mechanisms

**Explanation building**:
- What factors led to outcomes?
- How did mechanisms work?
- What alternative explanations exist?
- What evidence supports/refutes each?

**Example explanation**:
"Remote transition succeeded because: (1) pre-existing culture of documentation (evidence: Slack archives), (2) strong manager relationships before transition (evidence: interviews, retention data), and (3) intentional synchronous rituals established early (evidence: calendar analysis, observations)."

---

**5. Examine rival explanations** (1 day)

**Consider alternative explanations**:
- Could outcomes be due to something else?
- What evidence would refute your explanation?
- Test alternatives against evidence

**Strengthens validity** by showing alternatives were considered

---

### Phase 3 Outputs (Per Case)

- ✅ Chronology of key events
- ✅ Case narrative (rich description)
- ✅ Thematic analysis
- ✅ Explanation of outcomes (for explanatory cases)
- ✅ Rival explanations examined

---

## Phase 4: Cross-Case Analysis & Reporting

**Goal**: Compare cases (if multiple) and produce final case study report.

**Duration**: 1-2 weeks

---

### Cross-Case Analysis (Multiple Cases Only)

**Goal**: Identify patterns across cases

**Three strategies**:

**1. Case-ordered displays** - Arrange by key variable
- Order cases by outcome (high to low performing)
- Compare: What differs between high vs low?

**2. Thematic comparison** - Compare themes across cases
- Theme 1: How does it appear in each case?
- Theme 2: Present in all cases or only some?

**3. Causal network** - Map mechanisms across cases
- Factor X → Outcome: True in which cases?
- Replication logic: Does pattern replicate?

**Example cross-case finding**:
"Communication challenges emerged in all 5 cases (literal replication). However, 3 cases with pre-existing documentation culture adapted faster (theoretical replication: culture moderated effect)."

---

### Reporting Structure

**Single case study report** (15-25 pages):

1. **Introduction** (2-3 pages)
   - Research question
   - Case selection rationale
   - Case boundaries

2. **Literature & Theoretical Framework** (2-3 pages)
   - Existing knowledge
   - Theoretical propositions guiding study

3. **Methodology** (2-3 pages)
   - Data sources (triangulation)
   - Data collection procedures
   - Analysis approach
   - Limitations

4. **Case Description** (5-8 pages)
   - Context
   - Chronology of key events
   - Actors and actions
   - Rich narrative with evidence

5. **Analysis** (3-5 pages)
   - Themes and patterns
   - Explanation (for explanatory cases)
   - Link to research questions

6. **Discussion** (2-3 pages)
   - Findings in relation to literature
   - Theoretical contributions
   - Practical implications

7. **Conclusion** (1-2 pages)
   - Summary of key insights
   - Limitations
   - Future research

8. **References**

9. **Appendices** (Interview protocol, document list, chronology)

---

**Multiple case study report** (20-40 pages):

**Structure Option A: Integrated** (thematic across cases)
- Each analysis section discusses all cases together by theme

**Structure Option B: Case-by-case then integrated**
- Chapter per case (within-case analysis)
- Final chapter: Cross-case synthesis

**Choose based on**: Similarity of cases (integrated if similar, case-by-case if distinct)

---

### Validity and Reliability

**Four types of validity** (Robert Yin):

**1. Construct validity** - Using correct operational measures
- Strategy: Use multiple sources, establish chain of evidence, member checking

**2. Internal validity** - Establishing causal relationships (for explanatory cases)
- Strategy: Pattern matching, explanation building, rival explanations

**3. External validity** - Generalizability of findings
- Strategy: Replication logic (multiple cases), theoretical generalization (not statistical)

**4. Reliability** - Repeatable procedures
- Strategy: Case study protocol, database, chain of evidence

---

### Phase 4 Outputs

- ✅ Cross-case analysis (if multiple cases)
- ✅ Complete case study report (15-40 pages)
- ✅ Validity and reliability addressed
- ✅ Practical and theoretical implications documented

## Specialized Techniques

### Pattern Matching

**Purpose**: Compare empirical patterns with predicted patterns

**Process**:
1. Derive predictions from theory
2. Collect data
3. Compare: Do empirical patterns match predictions?
4. If yes → theory supported; if no → rival explanation or theory refinement

**Example**:
- **Prediction** (from theory): High psychological safety → more risk-taking behavior
- **Empirical**: In Team A (high safety), observed frequent experimentation; in Team B (low safety), observed risk aversion
- **Result**: Pattern matches, theory supported

---

### Time-Series Analysis

**Purpose**: Trace changes over time to infer causality

**Process**:
1. Establish chronology of events
2. Identify intervention or critical juncture
3. Compare patterns before vs after
4. Rule out alternative explanations (what else changed?)

**Example**:
- Before new manager (Jan-June): Team morale declining (weekly surveys)
- New manager starts (July)
- After new manager (Aug-Dec): Morale improving
- Alternative explanations: Company-wide bonus (but only for this team?), seasonality (but prior year showed different pattern)
- Conclusion: Manager change likely cause

---

### Logic Models

**Purpose**: Map inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes

**Use for**: Program or intervention case studies

**Example** (Onboarding program case):
- **Inputs**: Training materials, mentor assignment
- **Activities**: 2-week structured onboarding, weekly check-ins
- **Outputs**: 95% completion rate, high satisfaction
- **Outcomes**: Faster time to productivity, higher retention

**Helps**: Identify where program succeeds or fails

## Troubleshooting

### "Can't access key stakeholders or documents"

**Common in organizations** with confidentiality concerns or gatekeepers.

**Solutions**:
- Negotiate access upfront (before committing to case)
- Offer anonymity or confidentiality agreements
- Use public documents as alternative source
- Interview external observers (customers, partners, former employees)
- If access critically limited, reconsider case selection

---

### "Case boundaries are unclear or expanding"

**Symptom**: Scope creep, losing focus

**Solutions**:
- Return to research question: What are you trying to understand?
- Use time boundaries (e.g., focus on 6-month period, not entire company history)
- Use activity boundaries (e.g., focus on product launch, exclude unrelated initiatives)
- Document what's excluded and why

---

### "Too much data, overwhelming to analyze"

**Common with rich case studies** (interviews, documents, observations).

**Solutions**:
- Organize with case study database (structure prevents chaos)
- Start with chronology (timeline organizes events)
- Focus on research questions (ignore tangential data)
- Use software (NVivo, Atlas.ti) for large datasets
- Consider phased analysis (rough pass, then detailed pass)

---

## Known Limitations

**Case studies are not appropriate when**:
- Need statistical generalization (use survey)
- Want to test causal relationships definitively (use experiment)
- Can't access case or multiple evidence sources
- Phenomenon is not bounded or contemporary

**Case studies require**:
- Access to case (stakeholders, documents, observations)
- Multiple data sources for triangulation
- 3-16 weeks timeline (single to multiple cases)
- Tolerance for complexity and context-dependence

**Case studies excel when**:
- Need deep, contextualized understanding
- Phenomenon is contemporary and observable
- "How" and "why" questions
- Context is critical to understanding

---

## Integration with Other Methods

**Common combinations**:
- **Case Study + Survey**: Case study generates hypotheses, survey tests at scale
- **Case Study + Experiment**: Case study explains why experiment worked/failed
- **Case Study + Mixed Methods**: Case study is qual component of larger mixed methods design

**Within researcher skill**:
- Use UX research methods for interviews and observations
- Use evidence synthesis to situate case in literature
- Use mixed methods for multi-case replication designs
