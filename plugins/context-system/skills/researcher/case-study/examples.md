# Case Study Research Examples

**Purpose**: Real-world case study project walkthroughs across different case types and designs.

**Created**: 2026-02-08

## Example 1: Remote Transition Case Study (Single, Explanatory)

### Context

**Research question**: Why did Company X's remote transition succeed when many others struggled?

**Case type**: Explanatory (understand causal mechanisms)

**Case design**: Single case (unique success story, revelatory access)

**Timeline**: 6 weeks

**Boundaries**:
- Time: March 2020 - December 2021 (pandemic transition)
- Space: Engineering and Product org (120 people)
- Activity: Collaboration and culture practices (not performance outcomes)

---

### Phase 1: Case Selection & Design (Week 1)

**Why this case**:
- **Unique**: Company X achieved 95% employee satisfaction and zero regretted attrition during remote transition (industry averages: 65% satisfaction, 12% regretted attrition)
- **Revelatory**: CEO granted full access (interviews, documents, observations)
- **Theoretical interest**: Tests propositions about remote work success factors

**Data sources planned**:
1. Interviews: 15 stakeholders (CEO, managers, ICs across levels)
2. Documents: Internal emails, Slack archives, all-hands recordings
3. Archival: Employee surveys (quarterly), retention data, productivity metrics
4. Artifacts: Screenshots of tools, calendar analysis

---

### Phase 2: Data Collection (Week 2-4)

**Interviews** (15 participants):
- CEO + 3 executives
- 5 engineering managers
- 6 individual contributors (junior to senior)
- Questions: "Describe the transition", "What worked?", "What was hard?", "Why did it succeed?"

**Documents** (3 years of archives):
- 200+ internal emails about transition decisions
- Slack archives (#remote-work, #announcements)
- 8 all-hands meeting recordings
- Weekly manager meeting notes

**Archival data**:
- Quarterly employee surveys (engagement, satisfaction)
- Retention data (voluntary exits by quarter)
- Productivity proxies (story points, PR velocity)

**Observations**:
- Attended 4 remote team meetings
- Observed 2 virtual social events
- Reviewed 20+ async standups (written format)

---

### Phase 3: Within-Case Analysis (Week 5)

**Chronology of key events**:

```markdown
**March 13, 2020**: Office closure announced (24-hour notice)

**March 15-20**: Emergency "Remote Survival" week
- Daily CEO updates (email + Slack)
- IT shipped equipment to all homes
- Managers held 1-on-1s with every report

**March 23**: First all-remote all-hands
- CEO: "This will be hard, but we'll figure it out together"
- Introduced "remote working group" (cross-functional volunteers)

**April 2020**: Experimentation phase
- Teams trying different rituals (some worked, some didn't)
- Remote working group sharing what worked across teams
- Weekly "remote tips" Slack thread

**May 2020**: Codification phase
- Remote working group documented patterns that worked
- Company-wide "Remote Playbook" published
- Managers trained on async-first communication

**June-Dec 2020**: Refinement
- Quarterly surveys identified pain points
- Iterated on practices (e.g., added "focus time" calendar blocks)
- Established remote-first norms (cameras optional, async default)

**2021**: Stabilization
- Announced permanent remote-first policy
- Satisfaction scores returned to pre-pandemic levels
- Zero regretted attrition
```

---

**Thematic analysis** (from interviews + documents):

**Theme 1: Proactive Communication** (15/15 participants mentioned)
- CEO communicated daily in first month (transparency about uncertainty)
- Managers held frequent 1-on-1s (psychological safety)
- Public Slack channels for decisions (everyone could see reasoning)
- Quote: "I never felt in the dark. [CEO] was honest about not having answers but committed to figuring it out with us."

**Theme 2: Bottom-Up Innovation** (12/15 participants)
- Remote working group = volunteers, not appointed
- Teams experimented, shared what worked
- Best practices emerged from teams, then scaled
- Quote: "Management didn't dictate how to work remotely. They asked us what worked and codified it."

**Theme 3: Async-First Culture** (14/15 participants)
- Default to written communication (Slack, docs)
- Meetings only when necessary (synchronous = expensive)
- Work-life boundaries respected (no expectation of immediate response)
- Quote: "The shift to async was the biggest unlock. I could do deep work without constant interruptions."

**Theme 4: Trust and Autonomy** (13/15 participants)
- No surveillance or micromanagement
- Results-focused, not hours-focused
- Managers focused on unblocking, not monitoring
- Quote: "They trusted us to get work done. That trust made me want to prove them right."

**Theme 5: Intentional Rituals** (11/15 participants)
- Replaced spontaneous interactions with structured social time
- Weekly team socials (optional but well-attended)
- Monthly all-hands with "human moments" (not just business updates)
- Quote: "We lost hallway conversations, but we gained intentional connection time."

---

**Explanation building** (causal mechanisms):

**Research question**: Why did this transition succeed?

**Proposed explanation** (refined iteratively):

```
Success resulted from three reinforcing mechanisms:

1. **Trust → Autonomy → Accountability**
   - Evidence: No surveillance tools implemented (trust)
   - Evidence: Flexible work hours, async-first (autonomy)
   - Evidence: Productivity metrics stable/improved (accountability)
   - Mechanism: Trust empowered autonomy, which motivated accountability

2. **Bottom-Up Innovation → Psychological Safety → Adaptation**
   - Evidence: Remote working group = volunteers
   - Evidence: Managers encouraged experimentation
   - Evidence: Failures shared publicly as learning opportunities
   - Mechanism: Safety enabled experimentation, leading to rapid adaptation

3. **Proactive Communication → Reduced Uncertainty → Engagement**
   - Evidence: Daily CEO updates initially, frequent manager 1-on-1s
   - Evidence: Survey data shows "clarity" scores high despite uncertainty
   - Evidence: Retention remained high (no attrition spike)
   - Mechanism: Communication reduced anxiety, maintained engagement

Integration: These three mechanisms reinforced each other (virtuous cycle)
```

---

**Rival explanations examined**:

**Alternative 1**: "Success was due to pre-existing remote work practices"
- Evidence against: Company was 100% in-office before March 2020 (Slack archives, interviews confirm)
- Conclusion: Rejected

**Alternative 2**: "Success was due to nature of the work (software engineering)"
- Evidence: Other engineering orgs struggled with remote transition (industry data)
- Evidence: Success extended to Product org (less technical roles)
- Conclusion: Nature of work is facilitating factor but not sufficient explanation

**Alternative 3**: "Success was due to generous compensation/benefits"
- Evidence: Compensation did not change during transition (archival data)
- Evidence: Interviews cite culture factors, not compensation, as retention drivers
- Conclusion: Not primary explanation

---

### Phase 4: Reporting (Week 6)

**Report structure**:

1. **Introduction**: Research question, case selection (unique success story)

2. **Literature**: Remote work challenges, proposed success factors from prior research

3. **Methodology**: 15 interviews, documents, archival data, observations (triangulation)

4. **Case Description** (rich narrative):
   - Context: Pre-pandemic in-office culture
   - Transition: Chronology of March 2020 - Dec 2021
   - Key actors: CEO, remote working group, managers
   - Practices: Async-first, proactive communication, intentional rituals

5. **Analysis**:
   - Five themes with evidence
   - Three causal mechanisms explaining success
   - Rival explanations examined and rejected

6. **Discussion**:
   - Theoretical contribution: Trust, autonomy, and communication form reinforcing cycle
   - Practical implications: Playbook for successful remote transitions
   - Limitations: Single case (tech company, specific culture), cannot statistically generalize

7. **Conclusion**: Success not due to luck or industry, but to specific cultural practices and leadership decisions that can be replicated

---

### Key Learnings

**What worked**:
- Access to CEO and archives provided rich data (revelatory case)
- Chronology helped establish causal sequence (what led to what)
- Triangulation across sources validated findings (multiple perspectives converged)
- Rival explanations strengthened argument (showed alternatives were considered)

**What didn't**:
- Wish we'd interviewed employees who left during transition (survivorship bias)
- Single case limits generalizability (but replication logic in follow-up study could address)

**Outcome**:
- Case study published as "Remote Transition Playbook" for industry
- 3 other companies used findings to improve their remote policies
- Follow-up multiple-case study commissioned to test generalizability

## Example 2: Comparing AI Adoption Strategies (Multiple Cases, Descriptive)

### Context

**Research question**: How do companies approach AI adoption in product development?

**Case type**: Descriptive (rich description of phenomenon)

**Case design**: Multiple cases (5 companies, literal replication)

**Timeline**: 12 weeks

**Boundaries**:
- Time: AI adoption efforts in 2023-2025
- Space: Product development teams (not sales or support)
- Activity: How AI integrated into workflows (not outcomes)

---

### Phase 1: Case Selection & Design (Week 1-2)

**Selection criteria**:
- All 5 companies actively using AI in product development (literal replication: expect similar patterns)
- Variety in company size (startup, mid-size, enterprise) for richness
- Access to product leaders and developers

**Cases selected**:
1. **Startup A** (50 people, AI-native product)
2. **Startup B** (80 people, adding AI to existing product)
3. **Mid-size C** (300 people, experimenting with AI tools)
4. **Mid-size D** (500 people, building AI platform)
5. **Enterprise E** (5,000 people, regulated industry)

**Data sources** (per case):
- 3-5 interviews (product leader, 2-4 developers)
- Documents (AI strategy memos, tool adoption data)
- Observations (attend 1-2 product meetings)
- Artifacts (screenshots of AI tools in use)

---

### Phase 2: Data Collection (Week 3-8)

**Parallel case data collection** (6 weeks, staggered):

**Per case**:
- 3-5 interviews (45-60 min each)
- Document review (strategy docs, tool guides, Slack channels)
- Observation (1-2 product meetings or development sessions)
- Artifact collection (tool screenshots, workflow diagrams)

**Total across 5 cases**:
- 22 interviews
- 40+ documents
- 8 observations
- 35 artifacts

---

### Phase 3: Within-Case Analysis (Week 9-10)

**Case summaries** (one per case):

**Startup A** (AI-native):
- **Context**: Founded in 2023 specifically to build AI-powered product
- **Approach**: AI core to value prop, not add-on
- **Tools**: Custom-built AI pipeline, OpenAI API, fine-tuned models
- **Culture**: "AI-first" thinking, every feature considers AI integration
- **Challenges**: Model reliability, cost management, hiring AI talent

**Startup B** (Retrofitting AI):
- **Context**: Existing product (launched 2019), adding AI features in 2024
- **Approach**: AI as feature enhancement, not core redesign
- **Tools**: Off-the-shelf AI APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic), minimal custom training
- **Culture**: Debate about "real value" vs "AI hype", pragmatic adoption
- **Challenges**: Integration with existing architecture, user education

**Mid-size C** (Experimenting):
- **Context**: Exploring AI use cases, no committed strategy yet
- **Approach**: Bottom-up experiments by individual developers
- **Tools**: Mix of AI coding assistants (Copilot, Cursor) and API integrations
- **Culture**: Curiosity-driven, "let's try it and see"
- **Challenges**: Lack of coordination, unclear ROI, security concerns

**Mid-size D** (AI Platform):
- **Context**: Building internal AI platform for all product teams
- **Approach**: Centralized AI capabilities, teams integrate via platform
- **Tools**: Custom platform (model serving, monitoring, fine-tuning)
- **Culture**: "Platform enables, teams innovate", governance + experimentation
- **Challenges**: Platform adoption resistance, balancing control vs flexibility

**Enterprise E** (Regulated):
- **Context**: Heavily regulated industry (finance), cautious AI adoption
- **Approach**: AI for internal tooling first (customer-facing later)
- **Tools**: Vendor solutions with compliance certifications
- **Culture**: "Safety first, innovation second", extensive review processes
- **Challenges**: Regulatory compliance, risk aversion, slow decision-making

---

### Phase 4: Cross-Case Analysis & Reporting (Week 11-12)

**Cross-case patterns** (thematic comparison):

| Theme | Startup A | Startup B | Mid-size C | Mid-size D | Enterprise E | Pattern |
|-------|-----------|-----------|------------|------------|--------------|---------|
| **AI Strategy** | Core to product | Feature add-on | Experimental | Platform-first | Risk-managed | **Varies by context** (new vs existing product, size, industry) |
| **Build vs Buy** | Build (custom) | Buy (APIs) | Buy (APIs) | Build (platform) | Buy (vetted vendors) | **Size predicts build** (startups buy, mid-size+ build) |
| **Top-Down vs Bottom-Up** | Top-down (CEO-driven) | Bottom-up (eng experiments) | Bottom-up | Top-down (platform team) | Top-down (compliance-driven) | **Startups varied, larger = top-down** |
| **Primary Challenge** | Model reliability + cost | Integration complexity | Lack of coordination | Adoption resistance | Regulatory compliance | **Unique to context** |
| **Developer Adoption** | High (core to job) | Mixed (some enthusiastic, some skeptical) | High (individual tools) | Low initially (platform overhead) | Low (caution + bureaucracy) | **Friction increases with governance** |

---

**Key findings** (from cross-case synthesis):

**Finding 1: AI adoption strategy varies by product maturity**
- AI-native startups (A): AI is core value proposition
- Existing products (B, C): AI as enhancement or experimentation
- Platform orgs (D): AI as internal capability to enable teams
- Regulated (E): AI for internal efficiency before customer-facing

**Finding 2: Build vs Buy decision driven by scale and differentiation**
- Small companies (A, B, C): Buy APIs (faster, cheaper)
- Medium+ with AI as differentiator (D): Build platform (control, customization)
- Enterprise in regulated industries (E): Buy vetted vendors (compliance)

**Finding 3: Governance increases with company size and risk**
- Startups (A, B): Minimal governance, move fast
- Mid-size (C, D): Emerging governance (C=none, D=centralized platform)
- Enterprise (E): Extensive governance (legal, compliance, security reviews)

**Finding 4: Developer enthusiasm vs organizational friction**
- Individual developers excited about AI tools (coding assistants, productivity)
- Organizational AI adoption faces friction (integration, compliance, ROI uncertainty)
- Bottom-up adoption (C) high enthusiasm but low coordination
- Top-down adoption (D, E) high coordination but adoption resistance

---

**Report structure** (30 pages):

1. **Introduction**: Research question, case selection (5 companies across sizes/contexts)

2. **Methodology**: Multiple-case design, data sources per case

3. **Case Descriptions** (2-3 pages per case):
   - **Startup A**: AI-native approach
   - **Startup B**: Retrofitting AI
   - **Mid-size C**: Experimental adoption
   - **Mid-size D**: Platform strategy
   - **Enterprise E**: Regulated caution

4. **Cross-Case Analysis**:
   - Four key findings with evidence from multiple cases
   - Thematic comparison table
   - Patterns by company size, maturity, industry

5. **Discussion**:
   - No "one right way" to adopt AI (context-dependent)
   - Practical guidance: Choose strategy based on product maturity, scale, and risk tolerance
   - Theoretical contribution: AI adoption follows similar patterns to prior technology adoptions (cloud, mobile)

6. **Conclusion**: AI adoption playbook varies by context; replication logic confirmed variation is systematic, not random

---

### Key Learnings

**What worked**:
- Multiple cases revealed context-dependent patterns (single case would miss variation)
- Literal replication design (all adopted AI) allowed comparison of "how", not "whether"
- Cross-case table made patterns visually obvious

**What didn't**:
- 5 cases still limited (more cases would strengthen patterns)
- All cases were tech companies (generalization to non-tech unclear)
- Snapshot in time (2023-2025) - AI adoption still evolving

**Outcome**:
- Findings shared as "AI Adoption Playbook" with industry leaders
- 2 companies adjusted their strategies based on cross-case insights
- Follow-up longitudinal study tracking these 5 cases over time

## Example 3: Product Launch Failure Post-Mortem (Single, Explanatory)

### Context

**Research question**: Why did Feature X launch fail despite positive user research?

**Case type**: Explanatory (understand why failure occurred)

**Case design**: Single case (post-mortem of specific failed launch)

**Timeline**: 4 weeks

**Boundaries**:
- Time: 6-month period (3 months pre-launch, 3 months post-launch)
- Space: Product team (12 people) + user research team
- Activity: Feature development, launch, and post-launch response

---

### Phase 1: Case Selection & Design (Week 1)

**Why this case**:
- **Typical failure**: Positive research, poor adoption (common pattern worth understanding)
- **Access**: Product team willing to participate in retrospective
- **Learning opportunity**: Identify root causes to prevent future failures

**Data sources**:
- Interviews: 10 participants (PM, 2 designers, 4 engineers, 2 researchers, head of product)
- Documents: User research reports, PRDs, launch plan, metrics dashboards
- Archival: Usage analytics (pre/post launch), customer support tickets
- Observations: Post-mortem meeting

---

### Phase 2: Data Collection (Week 2)

**Interviews** (10 participants, 30-45 min):
- "Walk me through the feature development"
- "What did user research show?"
- "What was your hypothesis for launch?"
- "When did you realize it was failing?"
- "What do you think went wrong?"

**Documents**:
- 3 user research reports (problem validation, concept testing, usability testing)
- Product requirements document (PRD)
- Launch plan and go-to-market strategy
- Post-launch metrics reviews (weekly for 12 weeks)

**Archival data**:
- Usage analytics: Feature activation, engagement, retention
- Support tickets: 200+ tickets related to feature

**Observation**:
- Attended post-mortem meeting (2 hours, all 12 team members)

---

### Phase 3: Analysis (Week 3)

**Chronology**:

```markdown
**Month -6**: Problem discovery
- User research: "Current workflow is inefficient" (15/15 participants)
- Decision: Build Feature X to streamline workflow

**Month -5 to -3**: Design and testing
- Concept testing: 80% positive (10/12 users)
- Usability testing: 4.2/5.0 average rating (12 participants)
- Conclusion: "Strong signal, proceed to build"

**Month -3 to 0**: Development
- Engineering builds feature to spec
- QA testing (no major bugs)
- Beta testing with 10 power users (positive feedback)

**Month 0**: Launch
- Feature shipped to all users (12,000)
- Announced via email, in-app notification, blog post
- Initial excitement (high click-through on announcement)

**Week 1-2 post-launch**: Adoption lower than expected
- Activation: 18% (expected 50%)
- Support tickets: Confusion about how to use
- Team: "Give it time, adoption will grow"

**Week 3-8 post-launch**: Adoption plateaus
- Activation: 22% (barely growing)
- Engagement: 40% of activators use once, never return
- Support tickets: "Feature doesn't fit my workflow"

**Week 9-12 post-launch**: Crisis
- PM escalates to leadership: "Feature is failing"
- Post-mortem scheduled
- Discussion: Should we sunset or iterate?
```

---

**Explanation building** (Why did it fail?):

**Initial hypothesis** (before analysis): "Marketing and onboarding were insufficient"

**Evidence examined**:

**Evidence 1: User research reports**
- Problem: Research validated pain point ("inefficient workflow")
- But: Research focused on power users (5+ years tenure) who represented 8% of user base
- Insight: Problem was real but not widespread

**Evidence 2: Concept and usability testing**
- Testing scores were high (80% positive, 4.2/5.0)
- But: Testing participants were same power user segment
- Insight: Feature designed for minority, not majority

**Evidence 3: Beta testing**
- 10 beta users gave positive feedback
- But: All 10 were selected from power user segment (feature fit their needs)
- Insight: Beta didn't validate with broader user base

**Evidence 4: Usage analytics**
- 78% of users never activated feature (didn't click into it)
- Of 22% who activated, 60% used once and never returned
- Insight: Majority didn't see relevance, early adopters didn't find sustained value

**Evidence 5: Support tickets**
- Tickets showed confusion ("I don't understand when to use this")
- Tickets showed misalignment ("This doesn't fit my workflow")
- Insight: Feature solved problem most users didn't have

**Evidence 6: Interviews (post-launch)**
- PM: "We assumed all users faced this pain point"
- Researchers: "We recruited experienced users because they're easier to schedule"
- Designer: "We designed for the workflow we saw in research"
- Engineers: "We built what was in the PRD"
- Insight: Sampling bias in research cascaded through entire process

---

**Causal mechanism identified**:

```
Root cause: Sampling bias in user research

1. **Research sampled power users (8% of user base)**
   - Why: Easier to recruit (engaged, responsive)
   - Effect: Pain point identified was real but niche

2. **Product team assumed research was representative**
   - Why: Didn't question sample representativeness
   - Effect: Designed for minority use case

3. **Beta testing reinforced false confidence**
   - Why: Beta users were also power users (selected for engagement)
   - Effect: Positive feedback validated narrow solution

4. **Launch to broad user base revealed misalignment**
   - Why: 92% of users didn't have the pain point feature solved
   - Effect: Low activation, low engagement, feature failure

Mechanism: Selection bias created false signal → over-confident launch → predictable failure
```

---

**Rival explanations examined**:

**Alternative 1**: "Feature was buggy"
- Evidence: QA found no major bugs, beta users reported smooth experience
- Conclusion: Rejected (not a quality issue)

**Alternative 2**: "Marketing was insufficient"
- Evidence: High click-through on announcement (awareness was high)
- Evidence: Support tickets showed confusion, not lack of awareness
- Conclusion: Rejected (awareness existed, relevance didn't)

**Alternative 3**: "Onboarding was poor"
- Evidence: Usability testing showed 4.2/5.0 (interface was clear for those who needed it)
- Evidence: Analytics showed most users never tried to onboard (didn't activate feature)
- Conclusion: Partial (onboarding could improve, but wouldn't solve core relevance problem)

---

### Phase 4: Reporting (Week 4)

**Report structure** (post-mortem report for leadership):

1. **Executive Summary**: Feature failed because it solved a problem only 8% of users had (sampling bias in research)

2. **Chronology**: Timeline of decision points from research to launch

3. **Analysis**:
   - Root cause: Sampling bias (power users in research, beta)
   - Cascading effect: Narrow research → narrow design → broad launch → failure
   - Evidence: Usage analytics, support tickets, interviews

4. **Lessons Learned**:
   - **Lesson 1**: Validate sample representativeness before committing to build
   - **Lesson 2**: Estimate addressable user base (TAM for feature) early
   - **Lesson 3**: Beta test with diverse user segments, not just engaged users
   - **Lesson 4**: Challenge assumptions ("Do all users have this problem?")

5. **Recommendations**:
   - **Immediate**: Sunset feature (sunk cost, low adoption won't recover)
   - **Process change**: Add "sample representativeness check" to research process
   - **Future**: Estimate % of users affected by problem before design phase

6. **Appendix**: Interview quotes, analytics screenshots, support ticket examples

---

### Key Learnings

**What worked**:
- Post-mortem case study created psychological safety (no blame, just learning)
- Chronology revealed cascading decisions (each made sense at the time, but compounded)
- Explanation building identified root cause, not just symptoms

**What didn't**:
- Wish we'd interviewed users who didn't activate feature (would confirm "lack of relevance" hypothesis)
- Single case (can't generalize about all feature failures, but pattern is instructive)

**Outcome**:
- Feature sunsetted 6 months post-launch
- Product process updated: Research must validate sample representativeness
- New practice: "Addressable user base estimation" before design phase
- 2 years later, similar failures prevented by applying lessons

## Common Patterns Across Examples

### Pattern 1: Multiple Evidence Sources Validate Findings

All examples used triangulation:
- **Example 1**: Interviews + documents + archival data + observations converged on same themes
- **Example 2**: Cross-case comparison validated patterns weren't idiosyncratic
- **Example 3**: Analytics + support tickets + interviews confirmed sampling bias

**Lesson**: Single source is insufficient. Triangulation increases confidence.

---

### Pattern 2: Chronology Reveals Causal Sequence

All examples created timelines:
- **Example 1**: Chronology showed what led to success (trust → autonomy → accountability)
- **Example 2**: Timelines helped compare adoption stages across cases
- **Example 3**: Chronology revealed how early decision (biased sample) cascaded through process

**Lesson**: "What happened when" helps establish "what caused what"

---

### Pattern 3: Rival Explanations Strengthen Arguments

All examples examined alternatives:
- **Example 1**: Ruled out "pre-existing remote culture" and "compensation"
- **Example 2**: N/A (descriptive case, not testing causality)
- **Example 3**: Ruled out "bugs" and "marketing" as root causes

**Lesson**: Considering alternatives shows rigor, strengthens explanation

---

### Pattern 4: Rich Description Enables Transferability

All examples provided context:
- **Example 1**: Detailed company culture, transition timeline, practices
- **Example 2**: Described each company's context (size, industry, maturity)
- **Example 3**: Product team dynamics, research process, decision-making

**Lesson**: Readers can assess if findings apply to their context (transferability, not generalization)

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Anti-Pattern 1: Single Source Case Study

**Problem**: Relying on interviews alone, no document review or observations

**Better**: Use multiple sources (triangulation). Case studies require convergent evidence.

---

### Anti-Pattern 2: Unclear Boundaries

**Problem**: "Case is the company" (but which parts? what time period? what activities?)

**Better**: Define boundaries (time, space, activity). Clear scope prevents overgeneralization.

---

### Anti-Pattern 3: Description Without Analysis

**Problem**: Narrative of what happened, but no explanation of why or identification of patterns

**Better**: Description is necessary but insufficient. Always analyze (themes, causality, patterns).

---

### Anti-Pattern 4: Ignoring Rival Explanations

**Problem**: Propose one explanation, don't consider alternatives

**Better**: Examine rival explanations (even if you reject them). Strengthens your argument.
